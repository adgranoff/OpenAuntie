# Evidence Standards

OpenAuntie's knowledge base is curated from peer-reviewed research. Every claim must meet the standards described here.

## Citation Format

Every factual claim in the knowledge base must include an inline citation:

```
> [Claim text] [Author/Organization, Year, Publication] [Grade] [Reviewed: YYYY-MM]
```

**Example:**
```
> Children with consistent bedtimes display better emotional and behavioral regulation
> under stress compared to children with variable sleep times.
> [Penn State University, 2024, Journal of Developmental and Behavioral Pediatrics] [STRONG] [Reviewed: 2026-03]
```

## Evidence Grades

| Grade | Symbol | Criteria | Trust Level |
|-------|--------|----------|-------------|
| **STRONG** | `[STRONG]` | Multiple RCTs, meta-analyses (3+ studies), systematic reviews, or 10+ years of replicated findings | High confidence. Present as established knowledge. |
| **MODERATE** | `[MODERATE]` | Single well-designed RCT, validated program with published outcomes, or multiple correlational studies with consistent findings | Good confidence. Present as "research suggests" or "studies show." |
| **EMERGING** | `[EMERGING]` | Single correlational study, pilot program, small sample, or promising but unreplicated findings | Cautious confidence. Present as "preliminary research suggests" or "early evidence indicates." |
| **CONSENSUS** | `[CONSENSUS]` | Professional organization guidelines (AAP, APA, WHO), expert consensus statements, or widely adopted clinical practice | Institutional confidence. Present as "[Organization] recommends." |

## Review Dates

Each knowledge file includes a header:

```
# Topic Title
Last reviewed: 2026-03
```

Claims should be reviewed annually. When reviewing:

1. Check if the cited study has been superseded by newer research
2. Check if professional guidelines have been updated
3. If contradictory evidence exists, present both sides with grades
4. Update the "Reviewed" date on each verified claim

## Handling Contradictions

When research findings conflict:

1. Present both findings with their evidence grades
2. Note which is more recent
3. Note the methodological differences (sample size, study design)
4. Default to the higher-graded evidence
5. If grades are equal, note the disagreement honestly

**Example:**
```
> The relationship between social media use and adolescent mental health is actively debated.
> A 2024 meta-analysis found no correlation between social media time and mental health
> [Orben et al., 2024] [MODERATE], while longitudinal studies have found associations
> between heavy use and increased anxiety/depression [Twenge et al., 2023] [MODERATE].
> The AAP's 2026 policy recommends focusing on content quality and context rather than
> time limits alone [AAP, 2026] [CONSENSUS]. [Reviewed: 2026-03]
```

## Cultural Sensitivity

Research findings may vary across cultures. When a finding is primarily validated in Western/WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) populations:

1. Note this limitation
2. Include cross-cultural studies where available
3. Frame recommendations around universal goals (security, competence, belonging) rather than culturally specific methods

## Out-of-Scope Claims

The knowledge base must NOT include:

- Medical diagnoses or treatment recommendations
- Medication advice
- Therapeutic interventions requiring professional training
- Claims about specific children (only general developmental patterns)
- Legal advice about custody, guardianship, or child welfare
