# Example: New Baby Sibling

**Scenario:** The Torres family is expecting a new baby in 2 months. Their daughter Camila is 4 and they're worried about how she'll handle it.

---

## Two Months Before the Baby

> **Maria:** We're expecting a baby in about 2 months. Camila is 4 and she's our only child right now. She's very attached to me -- we're super close. I'm terrified she's going to feel replaced. What do we do?
>
> **Auntie:** First, the fact that you're thinking about this now -- two months out -- is really smart. Preparation makes a significant difference. And your instinct is right: Camila will have feelings about this, and some of them will be hard. But hard doesn't mean harmful, and with the right preparation, most kids adjust well.
>
> Here's the research-backed roadmap for preparing a 4-year-old for a new sibling. We'll take it in phases: before the baby arrives, the first week, and the first few months.
>
> **Phase 1: Prepare with honesty and age-appropriate expectations.**
>
> At 4, Camila can understand "there's a baby growing in Mommy's tummy" and "the baby will come home and live with us." What she cannot understand yet is what that will actually feel like. Don't try to sell her on how wonderful it will be. Instead, be honest and concrete.
>
> Good things to say:
> - "The baby will cry a lot. That's what babies do."
> - "Babies can't play right away. At first, the baby will mostly sleep and eat."
> - "Mommy will need to spend time with the baby, but you and Daddy will have special time together."
> - "Sometimes you might feel mad or sad about the baby. That's OK. You can always tell me how you feel."
>
> Things to avoid:
> - "You're going to LOVE being a big sister!" (Don't prescribe her feelings)
> - "You'll have a new best friend!" (Overpromising leads to disappointment when the baby can't play)
> - "You need to be a big girl now." (She's still 4. She doesn't need to grow up because you had another child)

> **Maria:** Should we involve her in getting ready?
>
> **Auntie:** Absolutely. Involvement is one of the strongest predictors of positive adjustment. Let her:
>
> - Help choose something for the baby's room (a stuffed animal, a blanket)
> - Come to a prenatal appointment and hear the heartbeat (if she wants to)
> - Help pack the hospital bag ("Can you pick out a blanket for the baby?")
> - Practice with a baby doll -- feeding, holding, being gentle
>
> The key word is "help," not "allow." Frame it as her contribution, not as you letting her participate. "I need your help picking out something soft for the baby" positions her as an important part of the process, not a bystander watching her replacement arrive.

---

## One Month Before: Setting Up Tracking

> **Maria:** I want to keep an eye on how Camila is doing emotionally through all of this. Should we start tracking now?
>
> **Auntie:** Great idea. Let's start mood tracking for Camila now, before the baby arrives. That gives us a baseline. If we can see what her emotional landscape looks like during a normal period, we'll be able to spot changes after the baby comes rather than guessing.
>
> Just check in once a day. What zone is she in (blue, green, yellow, red), and what's going on. It takes 30 seconds.

Over the next month, Maria logs Camila's mood daily.

> **Maria:** Camila was in the green zone today. Happy, played well at school, no issues.
>
> **Auntie:** Logged -- green, typical day. Keep going.

> **Maria:** Yellow zone today. She asked me "Will you still love me when the baby comes?" at bedtime. My heart broke.
>
> **Auntie:** Logged -- yellow, anxiety about parental love. And I know that question is heartbreaking, but it's actually a really healthy sign. She's able to name her fear and bring it to you. That means she trusts you with her hard feelings. A lot of kids hold that in.
>
> What did you tell her?

> **Maria:** I told her I would always love her, no matter what, and that getting a new baby doesn't mean less love -- it means more love in the family.
>
> **Auntie:** That's exactly right. And you may need to say it again. And again. And again. Children at this age need repeated reassurance, not because they don't believe you, but because the concept is abstract and the fear is visceral. Every time she asks and you answer with warmth and patience, you're depositing into her security account.

---

## The Baby Arrives

> **Maria:** The baby came yesterday! A boy -- Leo. Camila met him at the hospital. She was excited at first but then got really quiet. When we got home, she clung to me and asked if the baby could "go back."
>
> **Auntie:** Congratulations on Leo! And Camila's reaction is textbook normal. The excitement followed by withdrawal is one of the most common initial responses. She's processing something enormous -- the family structure she's known her entire life just changed permanently, and she has no control over it.
>
> "Can the baby go back?" is not a sign of rejection or cruelty. It's a 4-year-old's way of saying "I'm scared of what this means for me." Treat it with compassion, not correction. Don't say "That's not nice." Instead: "I know this is a big change. It's OK to feel confused about it."
>
> Here's what matters most in the first week:
>
> **1. Maintain Camila's routines.** Her bedtime, her meals, her rituals. When everything else is changing, routines are her anchor. If you had a bedtime routine going, keep it going -- even if it's Dad doing it instead of Mom for a few nights.
>
> **2. Protect one-on-one time.** Every single day, Camila needs at least 15-20 minutes of uninterrupted time with each parent where the baby is not present and not the topic of conversation. Play what she wants to play. Talk about what she wants to talk about. This is the single most important intervention you can make.
>
> **3. Let her regress.** If she starts baby-talking, wanting to be carried, sucking her thumb, or having accidents after being potty trained -- do not punish or shame her. Regression is the most common and most normal response to a new sibling. She's testing whether she's still allowed to need you. The answer needs to be yes. Regression almost always resolves on its own within a few weeks if met with patience rather than frustration.
>
> **4. Give her a role, but don't overdo it.** "Can you hand me the diaper?" is great. "You're Mommy's big helper now" as a constant refrain is too much pressure. She didn't ask for a job. She's still 4.

---

## Week 1: The Adjustment

Maria logs Camila's moods through the first week.

> **Maria:** Day 2 at home. Camila was yellow zone most of the day. She wanted to sit on my lap while I was nursing Leo and got upset when she couldn't. She said "You only care about the baby."
>
> **Auntie:** Logged -- yellow, feeling displaced during nursing. "You only care about the baby" is her translation of "I need you and you're not available right now." Both feelings are valid -- her need for you is real, and Leo's need to eat is real.
>
> Here's a small practical thing: keep a special basket next to your nursing spot with activities that are only available during nursing time. Books, stickers, a small toy. "This is our nursing time basket -- you can sit next to me and do these while I feed Leo." It gives her proximity and a special ritual rather than exclusion.

> **Maria:** Day 4. Camila had a full red-zone meltdown when David tried to do bedtime instead of me. Screaming, crying, "I want MOMMY." She hasn't done that in over a year.
>
> **Auntie:** Logged -- red zone, bedtime regression, separation distress. This is the regression I mentioned. She's not being difficult. She's checking: "When things get hard, will Mom still come?" Right now, if you can, go to her. This isn't about spoiling her. It's about answering the question she's really asking, which is "Am I still important to you?"
>
> Over the next few weeks, you can gradually transition bedtime back to shared duties. But in the acute adjustment period, follow her lead on which parent she needs.

> **Maria:** Day 7. She asked to hold Leo today. She was very gentle and said "He's soft." Then she went to play. Green zone for the first time since he was born.
>
> **Auntie:** Logged -- green zone, initiated physical affection with baby. That is a beautiful moment. She's starting to integrate Leo into her world on her own terms. She held him, she connected, and then she moved on to her own things. That's healthy adjustment in action.

---

## The First Month: Journal Entries

Maria starts journaling about the transition.

> **Maria:** Journal entry: "Week 2. Camila is definitely adjusting. She has good moments -- today she sang to Leo and it was the sweetest thing I've ever seen. But she also had a tantrum over the wrong color cup, which is something she hasn't done in a year. I think the small stuff is standing in for the big feelings she can't name. David has been amazing doing one-on-one time with her every evening after dinner. She looks forward to it. I'm exhausted but I think we're going to be OK."
>
> **Auntie:** Saved. And I want to point out something you wrote: "The small stuff is standing in for the big feelings she can't name." That is exactly right. At 4, she doesn't have the emotional vocabulary to say "I'm grieving the loss of being the only child and I'm scared of what my role is now." So it comes out as a meltdown about a cup. When you see through the surface behavior to the underlying emotion, you respond to the right thing.
>
> The singing to Leo is also significant. That's not just cute -- it's her beginning to see herself as having something to offer this new person. That's identity integration. She's starting to figure out who she is in this new family structure.

---

## Two Months Later

> **Maria:** It's been two months. Can I get a mood summary for Camila since Leo was born?
>
> **Auntie:** Here's Camila's mood trend over the past 8 weeks:
>
> | Period | Green | Yellow | Red | Blue |
> |--------|------:|-------:|----:|-----:|
> | 1 month before baby (baseline) | 82% | 14% | 0% | 4% |
> | Week 1 after baby | 14% | 57% | 29% | 0% |
> | Week 2 after baby | 43% | 43% | 14% | 0% |
> | Weeks 3-4 | 64% | 29% | 7% | 0% |
> | Weeks 5-8 | 75% | 21% | 4% | 0% |
>
> She dipped significantly in the first week (only 14% green), which is completely expected. By week 2, she was at 43% green. By weeks 5-8, she's at 75% green -- approaching her pre-baby baseline of 82%.
>
> The red-zone episodes dropped from 29% in week 1 to 4% by weeks 5-8. The regression is resolving.
>
> She's doing well, Maria. Really well. And that's because you did the hard things: you maintained routines, protected one-on-one time, allowed the regression without shaming it, and kept checking in on her feelings. The data confirms what your instincts were telling you.

> **Maria:** This makes me feel so much better. I was so worried we were messing her up.
>
> **Auntie:** You weren't messing her up. You were guiding her through one of the biggest transitions of her young life, and you did it with attention, patience, and love. The mood data doesn't lie -- she's coming back to baseline because she felt safe enough to have the hard feelings and come out the other side.

---

## What This Example Shows

1. **Preparation matters.** Starting the conversation and setting up tracking before the baby arrives gives you a baseline and a head start.
2. **Honesty over hype.** "The baby will cry a lot" prepares better than "You're going to love being a big sister!"
3. **Regression is normal, not alarming.** Baby talk, meltdowns, clinginess -- these are expected and resolve on their own when met with patience.
4. **One-on-one time is the intervention.** 15-20 minutes daily of uninterrupted individual attention was the highest-impact thing the family did.
5. **Mood tracking shows the arc.** Without data, the hard weeks feel endless. With data, you can see the recovery trajectory and trust the process.
6. **Journal entries capture what data cannot.** The qualitative moments (singing to Leo, "He's soft") are as important as the zone percentages.
7. **Maintain routines through upheaval.** When the family structure changes, routines are the child's anchor.
