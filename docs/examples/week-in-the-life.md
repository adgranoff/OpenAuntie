# A Week in the Life with OpenAuntie

This walkthrough follows the Martinez family through a full week of using OpenAuntie. Sarah is the primary parent. Her kids are Liam (8) and Mia (5). Her partner is David.

This is what day-to-day use actually looks like -- real questions, real conversations, real tracking.

---

## Monday: Getting Set Up

Sarah installs OpenAuntie on Claude Desktop and starts by setting up the family profile.

> **Sarah:** Help me set up my family profile. I'm Sarah Martinez, and I have two kids -- Liam is 8 (born 2018-02-14) and Mia is 5 (born 2021-06-30). My partner is David.
>
> **Auntie:** Welcome, Sarah! So glad you're here. Let me get everyone into the system.
>
> I've created the Martinez family profile with you, David, Liam (8 years 1 month), and Mia (5 years 8 months). Before we go further, can you tell me a little about each kid? What are their superpowers, and what's been hard lately?

> **Sarah:** Liam is really creative and loves reading, but he melts down over homework almost every night. He's also been fighting with Mia constantly. Mia is funny and social but she's been having trouble with bedtime -- she keeps getting out of bed.
>
> **Auntie:** Got it. Creative reader who melts down over homework and fights with his sister. Funny social butterfly who won't stay in bed. Both of those are extremely common at their ages, and both are things we can work on together.
>
> I've saved Liam's strengths (creative, loves reading) and challenges (homework resistance, sibling conflict), and Mia's strengths (funny, social) and challenges (bedtime difficulty). I also noted the sibling conflict since it affects both of them.
>
> Want to start with one of those, or should we set up some daily tracking first?

Sarah decides to set up a bedtime routine for Mia.

> **Sarah:** Let's create a bedtime routine for Mia. She needs to brush teeth, put on pajamas, pick out a book, read together, and then lights out. We start at 7:30 and it should take about 30 minutes.
>
> **Auntie:** Done! I've created Mia's bedtime routine with 5 steps:
>
> 1. Brush teeth (5 min)
> 2. Put on pajamas (5 min)
> 3. Pick out a book (5 min)
> 4. Read together (10 min)
> 5. Lights out (5 min)
>
> Target start: 7:30 PM, every day. Tonight when you run through it, just tell me how it went and I'll start tracking.

Sarah also sets up the behavior point system.

> **Sarah:** We want to try a point system for the kids. 3 points per day, and they can earn bonus points for extra kindness or effort. What do you think?
>
> **Auntie:** Three points per day is a solid starting point -- it's specific enough to matter but not so many that they lose meaning. I've set up the system at 3 points/day with a daily reset.
>
> A couple of research-backed tips: try to aim for a 5-to-1 positive-to-negative ratio in your interactions. That means for every time you deduct a point or give a correction, there should be at least 5 moments of praise, encouragement, or positive acknowledgment. The point system works best when it's mostly about catching them being good, not punishing them for being bad.
>
> Want to set up some rewards they can redeem points for?

## Tuesday: Starting to Track

Morning drop-off was hectic, but Liam earned a point for getting dressed without being asked. After school, homework was rough.

> **Sarah:** Liam earned a point this morning for getting dressed on his own without any reminders. But homework tonight was awful. He had math and it took 45 minutes with crying. I'd say the struggle level was a 2 out of 3.
>
> **Auntie:** Nice work, Liam! Point added for getting dressed independently -- that's exactly the kind of thing worth reinforcing.
>
> On the homework front -- 45 minutes of math with tears for an 8-year-old is a lot. I've logged it: math, struggle level 2, 45 minutes. For reference, the research-based guideline for a 3rd grader is about 30 minutes total across all subjects. If math alone is taking 45 minutes, something else might be going on -- we can dig into that if it keeps happening.
>
> How are you doing after that? Homework meltdowns are exhausting for everyone.

Later, Sarah checks in on Mia's mood.

> **Sarah:** Mia seemed kind of off today. Not her usual self. I'd say she was in the yellow zone -- a little anxious, maybe a 2 intensity. She kept asking if I was going to pick her up from school tomorrow.
>
> **Auntie:** Logged it -- yellow zone, intensity 2, some anxiety around school pickup. The fact that you noticed the shift and can name it is really good. Yellow zone means she's not in crisis but she's not in her comfortable green zone either.
>
> Has anything changed at school recently? New teacher, new classroom arrangement, a friend who moved? Sometimes at 5, separation anxiety can resurface without an obvious trigger. Keep an eye on it -- if it persists for more than a week or two, we might want to think about whether something specific is driving it.

## Wednesday: Sibling Conflict

The kids fight over a toy right after school.

> **Sarah:** Liam and Mia just had a huge fight over the TV remote. Mia wanted to watch her show and Liam said it was his turn. He grabbed it out of her hand and she screamed. I separated them but I don't know what to do.
>
> **Auntie:** Ugh, the post-school remote grab. Classic. I've logged the conflict -- Liam and Mia, trigger was TV remote dispute.
>
> Before we talk strategy: what happened right before the fight? Had they just gotten home from school?

> **Sarah:** Yeah, they literally walked in the door 10 minutes ago. They hadn't even had a snack yet.
>
> **Auntie:** That's a huge clue. After-school is one of the highest-conflict windows in any household. Kids have been holding it together all day at school -- following rules, managing social dynamics, sitting still -- and when they get home, they're running on empty. They need a decompression period before they can handle sharing, negotiating, or being flexible.
>
> Here's what I'd suggest trying tomorrow: when they walk in, don't turn on the TV right away. Give them each a snack and 10-15 minutes of separate, low-key time. Liam reads or draws. Mia plays independently. After that, they'll be in a much better state to negotiate TV time.
>
> For the resolution today -- did you end up mediating, or did it stay unresolved?

> **Sarah:** I gave them each 15 minutes of TV time. Mia went first because Liam grabbed the remote.
>
> **Auntie:** That's reasonable and fair. I've logged the resolution as mediated. For next time, research shows that for kids under 10, adult-mediated conflict resolution works much better than telling them to "work it out." You did the right thing by stepping in.
>
> One more thought: instead of "Mia goes first because Liam grabbed," try framing it as "Mia's turn is first today, and Liam's turn is after." Taking the punishment framing out of turn-taking reduces resentment. Liam probably already felt bad about grabbing -- he doesn't need the reminder that his sister's going first because of what he did.

## Friday: Reading and Screen Time

After school, Liam reads for 20 minutes.

> **Sarah:** Liam just read for 20 minutes -- he's on chapter 4 of "Diary of a Wimpy Kid." He seemed really into it, I'd say enjoyment was a 5.
>
> **Auntie:** Logged! 20 minutes of "Diary of a Wimpy Kid," enjoyment 5 out of 5. That's great -- when a kid is genuinely enjoying what they're reading, the reading takes care of itself. No need to push page counts or comprehension questions when they're in the zone.
>
> Fun fact: the Wimpy Kid series is one of the most effective "gateway" series for reluctant readers. If he's loving it, there are 18 books in the series, so you're set for a while.

Sarah asks about screen time.

> **Sarah:** We're having the screen time argument again. Liam wants to play Minecraft for hours and we don't know what's reasonable. What does the research say?
>
> **Auntie:** Great question, and the answer is probably not what you'd expect. The research has actually moved away from counting minutes.
>
> The American Academy of Pediatrics updated their guidance in 2026 to focus on the "5 C's" instead of time limits:
>
> 1. **Child** -- Consider Liam specifically. He's 8, creative, and Minecraft is actually a pretty solid creative/problem-solving game for his age.
> 2. **Content** -- Minecraft is construction, spatial reasoning, and planning. It's not the same as scrolling TikTok.
> 3. **Calm** -- Is he using Minecraft to cope with big feelings, or is it genuine recreation? Big difference.
> 4. **Crowding Out** -- This is the key question. Is Minecraft replacing sleep, physical activity, reading, family time, or outdoor play? If he's reading, playing outside, sleeping well, and doing homework, the Minecraft time is less concerning.
> 5. **Communication** -- Ask him about what he's building. Connect it to real-world concepts. Turn passive screen time into active conversation.
>
> If I had to suggest one high-impact change, it would be this: device-free meals. Research consistently shows that family meals without screens are one of the strongest predictors of child wellbeing across almost every measure. Start there, and the rest of the screen time picture becomes much easier to manage.

## Saturday: Family Time

The family goes to the park.

> **Sarah:** We just spent 2 hours at Riverside Park. Both kids came. Liam practiced on the monkey bars and Mia made a friend at the sandbox. I'd rate it a 5 out of 5 -- everyone had a great time. We'd definitely do it again.
>
> **Auntie:** That sounds like a wonderful Saturday. Logged it -- Riverside Park, 2 hours, both kids, rating 5/5, would repeat. Outdoor physical play is one of the most underrated interventions for everything from sleep quality to emotional regulation to sibling dynamics. A tired kid from the park is a completely different creature at bedtime than a kid who's been inside all day.

Sarah plans the family meeting for tomorrow.

> **Sarah:** We want to try a family meeting tomorrow. Can you help us plan the agenda?
>
> **Auntie:** Absolutely! Here's the auto-generated agenda based on this week's activity:
>
> **Martinez Family Meeting -- Sunday**
>
> 1. **Highs and Lows** -- Each person shares one good thing and one hard thing from the week
> 2. **Celebrations** -- Mia's 5-day bedtime streak! Liam's independent morning dressing and reading
> 3. **Discussion Items:**
>    - After-school routine (the TV remote situation -- how do we want to handle screen turns?)
>    - Homework approach (checking in with Liam about what would help)
> 4. **Upcoming** -- Any events, appointments, or plans for next week
> 5. **Family Fun Vote** -- What should we do together next weekend?
>
> Tip: keep it under 20 minutes. Start with the celebrations. Let the kids contribute to the agenda too -- they're more invested when they have a voice.

## Sunday: Reflect and Review

The family holds their meeting in the morning. Afterward, Sarah writes a journal entry.

> **Sarah:** The family meeting went really well. Liam suggested a "TV schedule" where they alternate who picks first each day, and Mia agreed. We also talked about homework and Liam said math is the hardest and he hates doing it when he's tired from soccer. I want to journal about this.
>
> **Auntie:** That's a great session. Both kids contributed real solutions -- that's exactly what you want from family meetings.
>
> I've saved your journal entry. And the detail about Liam and soccer before math homework is really useful. Let me keep tracking his homework sessions and we'll see if there's a pattern between soccer practice days and homework meltdowns. If there is, we might be able to solve the homework problem just by changing the order of his afternoon.

Sarah asks for the weekly summary.

> **Sarah:** Can I get a summary of the week?
>

> - Routines: 5-day bedtime streak! 100% completion, 0 resistance. Outstanding.

> - To watch: Separation anxiety pattern. If it continues, worth exploring.

> **Family**
> - 1 sibling conflict (TV remote, after school). Mediated successfully.
> - 1 family activity (park, rated 5/5).
> - First family meeting held -- both kids contributed to solutions.
> - Allowance paid on schedule.

## What Just Happened

In one week, the Martinez family:

- Set up their family profile with strengths and challenges for each child
- Created and tracked a bedtime routine (5-day streak)
- Logged behavior points, homework sessions, reading, and moods
- Tracked a sibling conflict and got evidence-based mediation guidance
- Got personalized screen time advice based on the AAP's latest framework
- Ran allowance through the three-jar system
- Held a family meeting with an auto-generated agenda
- Got a weekly summary that spotted a potential homework-soccer connection

None of this required filling out forms or learning a complicated app. Sarah just talked to Auntie about her day, and Auntie kept track of everything.
