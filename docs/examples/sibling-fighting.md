# Example: Sibling Fighting

**Scenario:** 6-year-old Mia and 9-year-old Liam fight constantly. Their dad David is losing his mind.

---

## Week 1: Asking for Help

> **David:** I need help. Liam is 9 and Mia is 6 and they fight about everything. Every single day. Who sits where in the car, whose turn it is on the iPad, who got more dessert, who's looking at who. I've tried telling them to work it out. I've tried separating them. I've tried taking things away. Nothing changes. I'm at the end of my rope.
>
> **Auntie:** I hear you, David. Constant sibling fighting is one of the most wearing things a parent can go through, because it never stops. It's not one big crisis you can solve -- it's a low-grade grind every single day. And it makes you feel like you're failing, even though you're not.
>
> A few questions before I weigh in:
>
> 1. When do the fights happen most? Is there a time of day that's worse?
> 2. Is one of them usually the instigator, or does it go both ways?
> 3. How do the fights typically end? Do you step in, does one kid give in, or does it just escalate until someone cries?
> 4. Are there times when they actually get along well? What are they doing when that happens?

> **David:** It's worst right after school and right before bed. Liam usually starts it -- he'll take something of Mia's or make fun of her. But Mia also provokes him by going into his room or touching his stuff. When I tell them to work it out, Liam usually wins because he's bigger and louder. Mia ends up crying. They get along OK when we're doing something active together, like at the park or playing a board game.
>
> **Auntie:** That's really helpful. Let me share some things the research says, and one of them might surprise you.
>
> **Stop telling them to "work it out."** I know that's the standard advice, but research by Perlman and Ross found that when you leave kids under 10 to resolve conflicts on their own, the bigger or more verbally skilled sibling almost always "wins." The younger child learns that their needs won't be protected, and the older child learns that dominance works. Neither outcome is good. For kids under 10, mediate -- don't leave them to figure it out.
>
> **Play-based interventions reduce conflict by 58%.** A study on sibling relationships found that cooperative play activities -- where siblings have to work together toward a shared goal -- significantly reduce the frequency and intensity of conflict. You already noticed this: they get along at the park and during board games. That's not a coincidence.
>
> **The after-school spike is real.** After a full day of managing behavior at school, kids come home emotionally depleted. Their capacity for patience, sharing, and flexibility is at its lowest point. The fights aren't about the iPad or the car seat. They're about being tapped out.
>
> Here's what I want to try: let's set up conflict tracking. Every time there's a fight, just tell me who was involved, what triggered it, what time it was, and how it got resolved. After two weeks, we'll have a map of their conflict pattern.

> **David:** OK, I can do that.

---

## Week 1: Tracking Begins

Over the first week, David logs 11 conflicts.

> **David:** Fight number one today. Right after school, 3:45 PM. Liam took the iPad from Mia while she was watching a video. She screamed. I stepped in, gave them each 15 minutes of separate iPad time.
>
> **Auntie:** Logged -- after school, iPad dispute, mediated with timed turns. Time noted: 3:45 PM.

> **David:** Another one. 7:15 PM. Mia went into Liam's room without asking and he pushed her out. She cried.
>
> **Auntie:** Logged -- pre-bedtime, boundary violation (Mia entered Liam's room), physical response (push). I'm noting that physical escalation happened -- we want to keep an eye on that.

The week continues. David logs conflicts as they happen:

- Tuesday 3:40 PM: Fought over snack portions
- Tuesday 7:30 PM: Argument over who brushes teeth first
- Wednesday 3:35 PM: Liam made fun of Mia's drawing
- Wednesday 4:00 PM: Fought over TV remote
- Thursday 3:50 PM: Mia took Liam's LEGO creation apart
- Friday 3:30 PM: Both wanted the same spot on the couch
- Saturday 10:00 AM: Argument during a board game
- Saturday 3:00 PM: Played well together at the park for 2 hours
- Sunday 11:00 AM: Fought over which movie to watch

---

## Week 2: More Data

David logs another 9 conflicts. By the end of two weeks, there are 20 logged conflicts.

> **David:** It's been two weeks. I feel like I've been logging fights nonstop. Please tell me you see something useful in all this.
>
> **Auntie:** I do. Let me show you the pattern.
>
> **Conflict timing:**
>
> | Time Window | Number of Conflicts | % of Total |
> |-------------|--------------------:|----------:|
> | 3:00 - 4:00 PM (after school) | 12 | 60% |
> | 7:00 - 8:00 PM (pre-bedtime) | 5 | 25% |
> | Morning / midday (weekends) | 3 | 15% |
>
> **60% of all conflicts happen in the first hour after school.** This is not a coincidence and it's not about the specific triggers (iPad, snacks, couch). It's about the time of day. They're emotionally depleted after holding it together at school all day, and the first hour home is when their regulation tank hits empty.
>
> **Pre-bedtime is the second hot zone (25%).** Fatigue stacks on top of the day's accumulated stress.
>
> **Weekends are significantly calmer.** Only 15% of conflicts happened on weekend mornings or afternoons, and one of those was a mild board game disagreement. When the kids are rested and not coming off a school day, they fight dramatically less.
>
> **Triggers:** The specific triggers don't matter much. It's not really about the iPad or the snack or who sits where. Those are proxies. The real trigger is depletion + proximity. Two tired kids in the same space will find something to fight about.

---

## The Plan

> **David:** So what do I do with this?
>
> **Auntie:** Here's a three-part plan based on what the data is showing:
>
> **Part 1: The After-School Buffer (targets the 60%)**
>
> When the kids get home from school, separate them for 15-20 minutes. Not as punishment -- as recovery. Give each kid:
> - A snack (hungry kids are angry kids)
> - Their own space (Liam in his room, Mia in the living room)
> - A quiet, individual activity (reading, drawing, solo screen time -- whatever they want)
>
> After 15-20 minutes of decompress time, they can share space again. You'll be amazed at the difference. A kid who's had a snack and 15 minutes alone is a fundamentally different person than a kid who walked in the door 2 minutes ago.
>
> Also: give each kid 10 minutes of one-on-one time with you during this window. It doesn't have to be structured -- just 10 minutes where each kid has your undivided attention. Research on attention-seeking behavior consistently shows that kids who get proactive one-on-one time seek less negative attention later.
>
> **Part 2: Cooperative Play Before Shared Screen Time**
>
> Before they're allowed to negotiate shared resources (TV, iPad), do one cooperative activity together. A quick board game, build something with LEGOs together, play catch in the yard. This activates the "teammate" neural pathways rather than the "competitor" pathways. The research showing 58% conflict reduction from play-based interventions was measuring exactly this effect.
>
> **Part 3: Mediate, Don't Referee**
>
> When conflicts do happen (and they will -- they're siblings), mediate instead of judging:
>
> 1. **Separate if physical.** Safety first, always.
> 2. **Hear both sides.** "Liam, tell me what happened from your perspective. Mia, you'll get your turn next."
> 3. **Restate each side.** "So Liam, you felt like Mia took your turn. And Mia, you felt like you'd been waiting a long time."
> 4. **Ask for solutions.** "What could we do so this works for both of you?"
> 5. **Don't determine who started it.** That question is unanswerable and reinforces a victim-aggressor dynamic.
>
> The goal is teaching them HOW to resolve conflict, not resolving it FOR them.

---

## Weeks 3-4: Tracking Improvement

David implements the plan. He continues logging conflicts.

> **David:** End of week 3. I've been doing the after-school buffer. Snack, separate decompress, 10 minutes one-on-one each. This week we had 4 conflicts total. Down from 11 in week 1.
>
> **Auntie:** That's a 64% reduction. And where did the remaining 4 happen?

> **David:** Two were after school (but milder -- bickering, not screaming). One was at bedtime. One was Saturday morning over breakfast.
>
> **Auntie:** The after-school conflicts dropped from 7 per week to 2, and the intensity dropped. The buffer is working. The fact that the remaining ones are bickering rather than screaming matters -- bickering is actually normal and even healthy sibling interaction. You're not trying to eliminate all conflict. You're trying to eliminate the destructive conflict.

> **David:** Week 4 update. 3 conflicts this week. One after school, two on the weekend. The after-school one was actually kind of funny -- they argued about who got to tell me about their day first. I just said "Mia goes first today, Liam goes first tomorrow" and it was over in 10 seconds.
>
> **Auntie:** That's wonderful, and that last one is a great example of a "healthy" conflict -- they both wanted your attention, not each other's stuff. And your response was perfect: quick mediation, no judgment, clear resolution.
>
> Let's look at the trend:
>
> | Week | Conflicts | After-School | Pre-Bedtime | Weekend |
> |------|----------:|-------------:|------------:|--------:|
> | 1 | 11 | 7 | 3 | 1 |
> | 2 | 9 | 5 | 2 | 2 |
> | 3 | 4 | 2 | 1 | 1 |
> | 4 | 3 | 1 | 0 | 2 |
>
> After-school conflicts went from 7 to 1. Pre-bedtime went from 3 to 0. Total conflicts dropped 73%. And the remaining conflicts are lower-intensity.
>
> The kids haven't fundamentally changed. What changed is the environment. You removed the conditions that were causing the fights (depletion + immediate proximity), and you replaced them with conditions that support regulation (snack, space, one-on-one time, cooperative play).

---

## What This Example Shows

1. **Tracking reveals timing patterns.** Without data, "they fight all the time" feels hopeless. With data, "60% of fights happen between 3 and 4 PM" gives you a specific lever to pull.
2. **The trigger is not the trigger.** It was never really about the iPad or the couch. It was about two depleted kids in close proximity.
3. **Separation is not punishment.** The after-school buffer is recovery time, not time-out. Framing matters.
4. **One-on-one time is an intervention.** 10 minutes of individual attention per child is one of the highest-return investments in the sibling dynamics research.
5. **The goal is not zero conflict.** Siblings will bicker. The goal is reducing destructive conflict and teaching resolution skills.
6. **Environment design beats behavior management.** Changing the after-school routine was more effective than any reward or consequence.
