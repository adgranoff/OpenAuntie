# How to Get the Best Advice from Auntie

## The Short Version

Auntie gives better advice when you give her more detail. The difference between a generic tip and a genuinely useful suggestion usually comes down to how specific you are with your question.

This guide shows you how to ask so you get answers that actually help.

---

## Be Specific

The more detail you share, the more targeted the advice.

**Vague:** "Bedtime is hard."

**Specific:** "Last night, Emma screamed for 20 minutes after I left the room. She kept saying she was scared. This has happened every night for the past week. She's 4."

The vague version gets general advice. The specific version gets advice tailored to Emma's age, the pattern of fear-based resistance, and the fact that it's a new behavior.

> **Try This**
>
> When describing a problem, include:
> - **What happened** (the actual behavior, as specifically as possible)
> - **When it happened** (time of day, what came before)
> - **How long it's been going on** (one time? every day for a month?)
> - **How you responded** (what you did in the moment)
> - **How it ended** (did it resolve? how long did it take?)

## Share Context

Auntie knows your family if you've set up a profile. But she doesn't know what happened at school today, or that your in-laws are visiting, or that your child just started a new medication.

Context changes everything.

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** Max has been really defiant this week. He won't do anything I ask without a fight.
>
> **Auntie:** That sounds exhausting. Before I jump in -- has anything changed recently? New school situation, family stress, health stuff, schedule changes? Sometimes what looks like defiance has a cause underneath.

> **You:** Actually, yes. His best friend moved away last week, and we're also in the middle of moving houses.
>
> **Auntie:** Oh, that changes everything. Max isn't being defiant -- he's grieving and stressed. Losing a best friend AND moving are both major events for a 7-year-old, and having them happen at the same time is a lot. The "defiance" is probably his stress and sadness coming out the only way he knows how right now.
>
> Here's what I'd suggest...

Without that context, you'd get advice about discipline. With it, you get advice about emotional support. The context made all the difference.

## Tell Her What You've Already Tried

This is one of the most important things you can do. If Auntie suggests something you already tried and it didn't work, that feels frustrating and generic. She always tries to ask first, but you can get ahead of it.

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** Emma won't eat anything but chicken nuggets and plain pasta. We've tried hiding vegetables in sauces, we've tried the "one bite" rule, and we've tried making her plate look fun. Nothing works. She's 4.
>
> **Auntie:** Good -- knowing what you've tried tells me a lot. The hiding-vegetables approach and the one-bite rule are both common first tries, and I'm not surprised they didn't land.
>
> At 4, the research actually says something most parents don't expect: pressure to eat (even gentle pressure like "just try one bite") tends to make picky eating worse, not better. The most effective approach is called "division of responsibility" -- you decide what's on the table, your child decides whether and how much to eat.

## Ask Follow-Up Questions

Auntie's first answer is a starting point, not the final word. Push for more.

**Useful follow-ups:**

- **"What if that doesn't work?"** -- Get a backup plan before you need one.
- **"Can you give me a specific script?"** -- Exact words you can say in the moment.
- **"What does the research say about this?"** -- When you want to understand the evidence behind the advice.
- **"Is this normal for a 6-year-old?"** -- Sometimes the most reassuring answer is "yes, completely."
- **"How long should I try this before changing course?"** -- Set expectations upfront.
- **"What should I NOT do?"** -- Knowing what to avoid is sometimes more helpful than knowing what to try.

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** You suggested I stop reminding Max to do his homework and let him experience the consequence of not turning it in. What if he just... doesn't care?
>
> **Auntie:** Fair question. Some kids genuinely don't care about the school consequence, at least not at first. Here's the thing -- the goal isn't the consequence itself. It's the shift in ownership. Right now, homework is YOUR problem because you're the one reminding and nagging. When you step back, it becomes HIS problem.
>
> If he consistently doesn't care after 2-3 weeks, that's useful information too. It might mean: the homework isn't at the right difficulty level, he's avoiding because he's struggling, or the motivation system needs something different. At that point, a conversation with his teacher would be my next step.

## Ask About Your Specific Child

Auntie knows your children if you've been sharing details. Use that.

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** Max got invited to a sleepover but he seems nervous about going. Should I make him go or let him skip it?
>
> **Auntie:** Based on what I know about Max, he tends to be cautious about new situations but usually has a great time once he's there. He was nervous about soccer camp too, and you told me he ended up loving it.
>
> I wouldn't force it, but I also wouldn't let him skip without a conversation. Try: "What part makes you nervous?" Sometimes it's a very specific fear (like what if he needs to come home at night) that has a practical solution (like knowing you'll pick him up anytime, no questions asked).
>
> What do you think is driving the nervousness?

## Use Auntie for Tough Conversations

Some of the hardest parenting moments aren't behavior problems -- they're conversations you don't know how to start.

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** How do I talk to my 8-year-old about divorce? My husband and I just decided to separate.
>
> **Auntie:** I'm so sorry you're going through this. This is one of the hardest conversations a parent can have, and the fact that you're thinking carefully about how to do it shows how much you love your kid.
>
> Here's what the research says works best:
>
> 1. **Tell them together** if at all possible. A united front, even in separation, gives children more security.
> 2. **Keep it simple and honest.** "Mom and Dad have decided we're not going to live together anymore. This is NOT because of anything you did."
> 3. **Answer the questions they actually ask.** An 8-year-old's first questions will be practical: "Where will I live? Will I still go to my school? Can I keep my room?"
> 4. **Reassure about what stays the same.** "We both love you exactly the same. You'll still see both of us. You'll still go to your school."
> 5. **Say it more than once.** This isn't a one-time conversation. He'll come back with new questions for weeks and months. Be available every time.
>

## Quick Reference: Good Questions to Ask

> **Try This**
>
> Keep these in your back pocket for when you need Auntie:
>
> - "Is this normal for age [X]?"
> - "What would you try first?"
> - "Can you give me exact words to say?"
> - "What does the research say about [topic]?"
> - "We've tried [X, Y, Z] and nothing works. What else can we do?"
> - "How do I talk to my [age]-year-old about [difficult topic]?"
> - "What patterns are you seeing in our data this week?"
> - "Should I be worried about this, or is it a phase?"
> - "What am I doing right? I need to hear something good today."
> - "Give me one thing I can try tonight."

## One Last Thing

Auntie is not a replacement for professional help. She'll tell you when something is beyond her scope -- and she won't sugarcoat it. If she says "I think this one deserves a conversation with your pediatrician" or "A family therapist could really help here," take that seriously. Knowing when to call in a professional is one of the most important things Auntie does.

But for the other 95% of parenting -- the daily questions, the pattern-spotting, the "I don't know what to do about bedtime" moments -- she's here. Be specific, share context, ask follow-ups, and don't be afraid to say "I'm struggling." That's what family is for.
