# Helping Kids Name and Manage Emotions

## Why This Matters: "Name It to Tame It"

Brain research shows something simple and powerful: when a child puts a feeling into words, the emotional intensity goes down. Brain scans actually show the "alarm center" of the brain calming when a person labels what they're feeling.

But here's the catch -- kids can only name emotions they have words for. A child who only knows "mad, sad, happy" has to cram everything into three boxes. A child who knows "frustrated, disappointed, embarrassed, worried, jealous" can be much more precise, and that precision helps them manage what they're feeling.

This is why daily mood tracking isn't just record-keeping. It's a skill-building exercise that teaches your child to notice, name, and eventually manage their emotions.

---

## The Zones of Regulation

One of the most helpful ways to talk about emotions with kids is through color-coded zones. These give children a simple framework that works across ages.

| Zone | Color | What it feels like | Example emotions |
|------|-------|--------------------|-----------------|
| **Blue** | Blue | Low energy, slow, down | Sad, tired, sick, bored, disappointed, lonely |
| **Green** | Green | Calm, alert, ready | Happy, focused, content, curious, grateful |
| **Yellow** | Yellow | Revved up, heightened | Frustrated, worried, excited, silly, anxious, nervous |
| **Red** | Red | Extremely intense | Furious, terrified, out of control, panicked |

### The Most Important Rule: No Zone Is "Bad"

This is critical. Every emotion is valid. Every zone is a normal place to be. The goal is not to be in the Green zone all the time -- that's impossible and unhealthy. The goal is to notice which zone you're in and know what to do about it.

Blue zone isn't bad -- sometimes you're tired and need rest. Yellow zone isn't bad -- sometimes you're excited and energized. Even the Red zone isn't bad -- it's a signal that you need help right now.

Never use zones as punishment. "Get back to Green right now!" defeats the entire purpose. Instead: "I can see you're in the Yellow zone. What do you need?"

## Daily Mood Check-Ins

Checking in on emotions should happen every day, not just when something goes wrong. If you only ask "How are you feeling?" during a meltdown, kids learn that feelings are only worth talking about when things are bad.

> **Try This**
>
> Pick a consistent moment each day for a mood check-in. Good times include:
>
> - After school ("What zone are you in right now?")
> - At dinner ("What was one thing that put you in the Yellow zone today?")
> - Before bed ("What zone were you in most of the day?")
>
> Keep it casual. Keep it short. Even a 30-second check-in counts.
>
> Model it yourself: "I was in the Yellow zone this afternoon because work was stressful. I took some deep breaths and got back to Green." When kids hear you name YOUR emotions, they learn it's safe to name theirs.

## Logging Moods with OpenAuntie

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** Log mood for Emma -- she's in the Yellow zone, feeling frustrated about homework.
>
> **Auntie:** Got it. I've logged Emma in the Yellow zone today -- frustrated about homework.
>
> This is the third Yellow-zone day this week, and homework has been the trigger each time. That's a pattern worth paying attention to. Is the homework itself too hard, or is it something about the timing or environment?
>
> When she's calm, you might ask her: "What's the hardest part about homework for you right now?" Sometimes kids can name the specific thing that's frustrating if we ask at the right moment.

> **You:** Log mood for Max -- he's in the Green zone. Good day.
>
> **Auntie:** Logged! Max has been in the Green zone 4 out of 5 days this week. That's a great streak. Worth mentioning to him -- "Hey, you've had a really solid week. What do you think helped?" Building his awareness of what keeps him in the Green zone is just as valuable as managing the tough days.

## Coping Strategies by Zone

Once kids can identify their zone, they need tools for what to do in each one.

### Blue Zone (low energy, down)
The goal: gently increase energy or mood.

- Movement: stretching, a short walk, jumping jacks
- Connection: a hug, talking to someone, playing with a pet
- Sensory: cold water on the face, a crunchy snack, change of scenery
- Small goals: accomplish one easy thing to build momentum

### Green Zone (calm, alert)
The goal: notice it, appreciate it, keep going.

- This is the sweet spot. Enjoy it.
- Point it out: "You seem really calm and focused right now. How does that feel?"

### Yellow Zone (stressed, frustrated, anxious)
The goal: slow down before it escalates to Red.

- Deep breathing: breathe in for 4 counts, out for 6
- Counting backward from 10
- Self-talk: "I can handle this. I'm going to be okay."
- Physical: squeeze a stress ball, push against a wall, take a walk
- Space: go to a calm-down spot for a few minutes

### Red Zone (intense, out of control)
The goal: get safe, get calm. Teaching happens later.

- Stop and remove from the situation if needed
- An adult provides calm, steady presence (lower your voice, slow your breathing)
- Physical release: stomp feet, squeeze a pillow
- Grounding: name 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you smell, 1 you taste
- NO teaching, no lectures, no problem-solving until the child is back in Yellow or Green

> **Try This**
>
> Make a "coping menu" with your child. Write or draw strategies for each zone on a poster. Put it on the wall.
>
> When they're calm (Green zone), practice the strategies together. Role-play. Try the breathing exercises. The time to learn these tools is NOT during a meltdown -- it's before one happens.

## Building Emotional Vocabulary

Kids need more words than "mad, sad, happy." Here's a rough guide by age:

**Ages 3-5:** Happy, sad, mad, scared, excited, tired, silly, frustrated

**Ages 5-8:** Add: disappointed, worried, embarrassed, jealous, lonely, proud, surprised, confused, nervous, overwhelmed, grateful, bored, hopeful

**Ages 8-12:** Add: anxious, humiliated, betrayed, resentful, conflicted, vulnerable, insecure, determined, guilty, ashamed, envious, content

**Ages 12+:** Add: apprehensive, exasperated, wistful, compassionate, mortified, resigned, indignant, pensive

You don't need to teach these like a vocabulary test. Just use them in conversation. "You look disappointed" instead of "You look sad." "I'm feeling a bit overwhelmed" instead of "I'm stressed." The more emotional words your child hears, the more they'll use.

## When to Seek Professional Help

Daily mood tracking sometimes reveals patterns that point to something bigger. Talk to your child's doctor or a therapist if:

- Your child is in the Blue zone most days for two weeks or more (persistent sadness, withdrawal, loss of interest)
- Anxiety is interfering with daily life (refusing school, avoiding friends, physical symptoms like stomachaches before events)
- Anger is intense, frequent, and not responding to any coping strategies after consistent effort for several weeks
- Your child talks about not wanting to be alive, hurting themselves, or feeling like the family would be better without them -- this needs immediate professional attention

Auntie will flag these patterns if she sees them in your logs. Noticing something early and getting the right support is not a failure -- it's exactly what a good parent does.

## The Long Game

Emotional intelligence isn't built in a week. It's built through hundreds of small moments: the check-in at dinner, the "what zone are you in?" on the car ride home, the time you said "I'm in the Yellow zone right now" and showed your child how you handled it.

Every time your child names a feeling, their brain gets a little better at managing that feeling. Every time you validate an emotion ("That sounds really frustrating") instead of dismissing it ("It's not a big deal"), you're teaching them that feelings are safe to have and talk about.

That's the work. It's small, it's daily, and it adds up.
