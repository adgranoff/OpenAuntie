# Running Family Meetings with OpenAuntie

## Why Family Meetings Work

Family meetings sound formal. They're not. Think of it as a weekly sit-down where everyone gets a voice, problems get solved before they blow up, and you plan something fun together.

The research shows that families who hold regular meetings report less fighting, better cooperation from kids, and a stronger sense of belonging. Kids who feel like they have a real voice in family decisions are more likely to follow through on those decisions.

The key word is "regular." A meeting once when things are bad is a crisis intervention. A meeting every week -- even when things are fine -- is what builds the habit of talking, listening, and solving problems together.

## The 7-Part Agenda

A structured agenda keeps the meeting from turning into a complaint session or a parent lecture. Here's the format that works:

### 1. Appreciations and Compliments
Every meeting starts here. Each person says something they appreciated about another family member this week.

- "I want to appreciate Max for helping me find my keys on Thursday."
- "I want to thank Mom for making my favorite dinner."

This sets a positive tone and trains everyone to notice the good stuff.

### 2. Calendar Review
What's coming up this week? Games, appointments, schedule changes, playdates. This gives kids a sense of what to expect and eliminates "I didn't know about that!" surprises.

### 3. Old Business
Follow up on decisions from last week. Did the new chore schedule work? Did the bedtime change help? This teaches accountability and shows that decisions made in the meeting actually matter.

### 4. New Business
Raise new issues, suggestions, or decisions. This is where problems get solved. The rule: brainstorm solutions together. Write every idea down without judging it. Then pick one the whole family can live with.

### 5. Chore Review
How are household responsibilities going? Does the chore split feel fair? Does anything need to rotate? Keep this matter-of-fact, not punitive.

### 6. Fun Planning
Plan something enjoyable for the week: game night, a park visit, a special meal, a movie. This is not optional. If meetings are all business, kids will dread them.

### 7. Closing Ritual
End the same way every time: a family cheer, a group hug, sharing dessert, or a quick game. The ritual signals that the meeting is something to look forward to.

## How Long Should It Be?

| Age of youngest child | Duration | Notes |
|----------------------|----------|-------|
| 3-5 years | 10-15 minutes | Keep it very simple: appreciations, one problem, fun planning |
| 6-10 years | 15-20 minutes | Full agenda works. Kids can start helping run the meeting |
| 11+ years | 20-30 minutes | Teens can handle longer discussions. Respect their input |

Start short. You can always go longer as the family gets used to it.

## Ground Rules That Work

> **Try This**
>
> Before your first meeting, establish these ground rules. Post them where everyone can see:
>
> 1. **One person talks at a time.** Use a "talking object" for younger kids -- whoever holds the special rock (or spatula, or stuffed animal) gets to speak.
> 2. **Everyone gets heard.** No interrupting. No eye-rolling. No "that's a dumb idea."
> 3. **We focus on solutions, not blame.** "How do we fix this?" not "Whose fault is this?"
> 4. **No screens.** Phones, tablets, and TV off for everyone -- parents included.
> 5. **This is a safe space.** Nothing shared in the meeting gets used against someone later.
> 6. **Decisions need everyone's agreement.** Not a vote where majority wins -- everyone has to be able to live with it.

## Getting Reluctant Kids Engaged

"We're having a family meeting" is not going to get cheers, especially at first. Here's how to build buy-in:

**Serve snacks.** Special meeting snacks -- popcorn, cookies, fruit plate -- create positive associations. This alone can shift the mood.

**Always include fun planning.** If kids know they get to help pick the weekend activity, they'll show up.

**Rotate the meeting leader.** Even a 5-year-old can call on the next speaker or "read" the agenda (with help). Older kids can fully facilitate. Being in charge makes it feel like THEIR meeting, not another parent lecture.

**Follow through on decisions.** Nothing kills the meeting faster than making a decision that never happens. If the family voted on taco Tuesday, taco Tuesday happens.

**Keep it short at first.** A 45-minute meeting with squirming kids is worse than a focused 12-minute meeting that ends on a high note. Build duration gradually.

## A Between-Meeting Trick: The Collection Box

Put a jar, basket, or box somewhere visible. During the week, anyone can write down something they want to bring up at the next meeting and drop it in.

This does two things:
1. It gives kids a way to raise concerns that feel urgent without needing to resolve them on the spot. "That sounds like something for the meeting box" is a powerful way to defuse in-the-moment conflict.
2. It teaches everyone that their concerns will be heard -- just at the right time and place.

## Your First Meeting

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** We've never done a family meeting before. How do I start?
>
> **Auntie:** Here's a simple plan for your first one:
>
> 1. Pick a consistent day and time (Sunday evening works well for many families).
> 2. Call it something fun if "family meeting" sounds too formal. Some families use "team huddle" or "the huddle."
> 3. For the first meeting, keep it to 3 things: appreciations, one fun plan for the week, and a closing ritual.
> 4. Bring snacks.
> 5. Don't try to solve big problems in the first meeting. Let everyone get comfortable with the format.
> 6. At the end, ask: "What did you think? Should we do this again next week?"
>
> The goal of meeting one is to make everyone want meeting two.

## When It Feels Like It's Not Working

If your first few meetings feel awkward or short, that's normal. Like any new routine, it takes about a month to feel natural.

If teens resist, give them a genuine voice. Adolescents can smell token participation a mile away. If their ideas get shot down every week, they'll stop coming. Let them influence real decisions.

If meetings devolve into arguments, go back to the ground rules. Use the talking object. Remind everyone: solutions, not blame. If a topic is too heated, table it: "Let's think about this during the week and come back to it next meeting."

Auntie can help you debrief after a rocky meeting and adjust the format for next time.
