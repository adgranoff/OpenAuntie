# Setting Up the Three-Jar Allowance System

## When to Start

Most kids are ready for a simple allowance around age 5 or 6. The signs are clear:

- They know a quarter is worth more than a nickel
- They ask "Can we buy that?" or "How much does it cost?"
- They can count and understand "more" and "less"
- They show interest when you pay at the store

If your child is doing any of these, they're ready. And there's good reason to start sooner rather than later -- research from Cambridge University found that children's money habits are largely formed by age 7.

---

## The Three Jars

The system is simple: every time your child gets money, it goes into three labeled jars.

| Jar | Purpose |
|-----|---------|
| **Save** | For something bigger that they're working toward |
| **Spend** | For whatever they want, whenever they want |
| **Give** | For helping others -- charity, community, or gifts |

Use clear jars (mason jars work great) so your child can actually see the money growing. That visual is a big part of why this works.

### Choosing a Split

There's no single right answer. Pick a split that matches your family's values:

| Split | Save / Spend / Give | Good for |
|-------|---------------------|----------|
| 50 / 40 / 10 | Heavy saving emphasis | Families who want to prioritize saving |
| 40 / 40 / 20 | Balanced with generous giving | Families who emphasize generosity |
| 33 / 33 / 33 | Equal across the board | Simple, balanced approach |

---

## How Much Per Week

A common guideline: **$1 per year of age per week.** So a 6-year-old gets $6, a 10-year-old gets $10.

| Age | Weekly amount |
|-----|--------------|
| 5 | $5 |
| 7 | $7 |
| 10 | $10 |
| 13 | $13 |

The exact amount matters less than having a system. A child who learns to manage $3 a week with structure is getting a better education than a child who gets $20 with no plan.

---

## The Hybrid Model (Recommended)

There are different views on whether allowance should be tied to chores. The approach that works best for most families separates them:

**Base chores are unpaid.** Making the bed, clearing dishes, putting away laundry -- these are things you do because you're part of the family. Not because you're getting paid.

**Extra chores earn money.** Washing the car, weeding the garden, organizing the garage -- these are optional jobs that go above and beyond.

**The allowance itself is a learning tool.** It comes every week regardless, so your child can practice managing money consistently.

This way your child learns two things at once: contributing to the family is expected AND working hard can earn extra.

## Setting It Up with OpenAuntie

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** Set up allowance for Max at $7 per week. He's 7. I want to do the three-jar system with a 50/40/10 split.
>
> **Auntie:** Done! Here's Max's allowance setup:
>
> **Weekly amount:** $7
> **Save jar:** $3.50 (50%)
> **Spend jar:** $2.80 (40%)
> **Give jar:** $0.70 (10%)
>
> A few suggestions to make this work:
>
> 1. Have Max physically divide the money himself each week. The act of putting coins into jars is part of the learning.
> 2. Write his savings goal on the Save jar. "What are you saving for?" Even if it changes, having a visible goal keeps him motivated.
> 3. Let him pick where the Give jar money goes. His choice -- a cause he cares about.
> 4. Most importantly: when the Spend jar is empty, it's empty. Don't bail him out. That's the lesson.
>
> Want me to also track base chores and extra earning opportunities?

> **You:** Yes, what should his base chores be?
>
> **Auntie:** For a 7-year-old, reasonable base chores (unpaid, expected) would be:
> - Make bed each morning
> - Clear plate after meals
> - Put dirty clothes in the hamper
> - Help set the table
>
> Extra earning opportunities (optional, paid) could be:
> - Help wash the car -- $3
> - Rake leaves or pull weeds -- $2
> - Organize a closet or drawer -- $2
> - Help with a bigger cleaning project -- $3-5
>
> These are just starting points. What feels right for your family?

## Let Them Make Mistakes

This is the hardest part for parents. When your child blows their entire Spend jar on a cheap toy that breaks the next day, **that is the point.**

> **Try This**
>
> When the Spend jar is empty:
>
> - Do NOT refill it. Do not lend against next week. Do not buy the thing they want.
> - Say: "I know it's disappointing. Your Spend jar is empty right now. What could you do differently next time?"
> - Let them sit with the feeling of having no money. This is how budgeting lessons actually land.
>
> When they buy something junky:
>
> - Don't say "I told you so."
> - Do say: "What do you think about that purchase now? Would you choose differently?"
> - They'll remember the broken $8 toy longer than any lecture about quality vs. price.

The research is clear: hands-on experience with money teaches children far more than talking about money ever will. Mistakes are features of this system, not bugs.

## The Save Jar: Making It Work

The Save jar needs a specific goal written on it. "Save" is too vague. "Lego spaceship -- $45" gives your child something to track toward.

- Celebrate milestones: "Your Save jar has $20! You're almost halfway there."
- Help them do the math: "If you save $3.50 a week, how many weeks until you reach $45?"
- When they reach the goal, make the purchase a big deal. They earned this.

For older kids (10+), consider opening a real savings account at a bank or credit union. Many have youth accounts. Seeing a balance on a real statement is powerful.

---

## The Give Jar: Building Generosity

Let your child choose where the Give jar money goes. This is their decision, not yours.

- Some kids want to help animals. Some care about hungry people. Some want to buy a gift for a friend.
- When the Give jar has enough, deliver the donation together if possible. Drop off cans at the food bank. Hand the money to the person collecting for the charity. Making it tangible makes it meaningful.
- Donate regularly -- monthly or quarterly -- so the Give jar doesn't just sit there forever.

## Age Adjustments

**Ages 5-7:** Physical jars with real coins and bills. Keep it tactile. Let them pay at the store and count change.

**Ages 8-10:** Jars still work, but introduce the idea of interest ("Your money can make more money over time"). Start involving them in grocery shopping and price comparison.

**Ages 11-13:** Transition from jars to envelopes or a simple app. Give them responsibility for a real budget category (their school supplies, their entertainment). Teach needs vs. wants.

**Ages 14+:** Move to a supervised debit card. Real banking, real budgeting. Start conversations about credit, debt, and investing. If they have a job, help them understand their paycheck.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**"My kid spends everything immediately."**
Perfect. Let them. The empty Spend jar teaches budgeting faster than any conversation. If it continues, gently ask: "I notice your Spend money is gone by Tuesday every week. What do you think is happening?" Let them figure it out.

**"Is the amount too little / too much?"**
The system matters more than the number. Adjust based on what your child is expected to buy with it and what your family budget allows.

**"Should I match their savings?"**
Great idea for kids 8+. Matching savings (even at 25 or 50 cents on the dollar) teaches the concept of employer matching and incentivizes saving. Just be consistent.

**"What if they refuse to put money in the Give jar?"**
Have a conversation, not a mandate. Talk about why giving matters to your family. Let them choose a cause they care about. If they genuinely resist, start with a smaller Give percentage and revisit later.
