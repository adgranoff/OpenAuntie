# Setting Up a Point and Reward System

## When Points Help (and When They Don't)

Point systems work well when your child needs help building a habit they find hard or unpleasant. Getting dressed without a battle. Brushing teeth. Staying in bed after lights out. These are things your child isn't doing yet, and a little external motivation can bridge the gap until the habit sticks on its own.

Points do NOT work well for things your child already enjoys. If your kid loves drawing and you start giving them stars for it, you can actually make them less interested over time. The research on this is surprisingly clear: rewarding something a child already does willingly can backfire.

**The rule of thumb:** Use points for building new, challenging habits. Skip them for things that are already going well.

---

## Setting Things Up

### How Many Points?

Keep it simple. The younger the child, the fewer points.

| Age | Points per day | How fast they earn a reward |
|-----|---------------|----------------------------|
| 3-5 | 3-5 | Same day or next day |
| 5-8 | 5-10 | Within 1-3 days |
| 8-12 | 10-15 | Within a week |
| 12+ | Use levels/privileges instead of points | Within 1-2 weeks |

### What to Track?

Start with ONE to THREE behaviors. Not ten. Not five. One to three.

Pick things that are specific and observable. "Be good" is too vague. "Put shoes on before the timer goes off" is perfect.

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** Set up a point system with 3 points per day for Max. He needs to get dressed by himself, put his plate in the sink after meals, and brush teeth without being asked.
>
> **Auntie:** Great choices -- those are all specific and observable. Here's what I've set up for Max:
>
> **Max's Daily Points (3 available)**
> 1. Gets dressed independently
> 2. Clears plate after meals
> 3. Brushes teeth without reminders
>
> A couple of things to make this work: always pair the point with specific praise. Don't just add a point silently -- say "Max, you earned a point because you got dressed all by yourself. Nice work!" That praise is actually the part that builds the long-term habit.
>
> What rewards does Max want to work toward? It's really important that he helps pick them.

## Choosing Rewards

Let your child help pick the rewards. This is not optional -- it's one of the biggest factors in whether the system works. Kids who choose their own rewards are more invested.

Start small. If you begin with a trip to the theme park, you have nowhere to go.

> **Try This**
>
> Make a reward menu together. Write down options at three levels:
>
> **Small (3-5 points):** Extra story at bedtime, pick what's for dinner, 15 extra minutes of play before bed
>
> **Medium (10-15 points):** Friend sleepover, special outing with one parent, pick a family movie
>
> **Big (25-30 points):** Bigger experience, a desired toy, a special day trip
>
> Stick the menu on the fridge so everyone can see it.

---

## The Golden Rules

1. **Give points right away.** Don't wait until bedtime to count up the day. Hand them out the moment the behavior happens. Especially for kids under 6 -- they need the connection to be immediate.

2. **Always say WHY.** "You earned a point because you put your shoes on the first time I asked." Never hand out a point silently.

3. **Never take points away.** Points are earned, not lost. Taking away points turns the system into a punishment, and research shows that doesn't work nearly as well. If your child misbehaves, handle it separately.

4. **Be consistent.** Every caregiver in the house uses the same system, the same rules, the same criteria. Inconsistency is the number one reason point systems fail.

## The Fading Plan: When to Phase Out

The whole point of a point system is to make it unnecessary. You're building a bridge, not a permanent road.

**Signs your child is ready to move on:**
- They do the behavior without being reminded
- They do it at school, at grandma's house, when no one is watching
- They show pride in the behavior on their own ("Look, I already brushed my teeth!")
- The chart starts to feel like a formality

**How to fade:**
1. Space out the rewards (from every day to every few days)
2. Keep the verbal praise at full strength even as points slow down
3. Eventually, drop the points entirely but keep noticing and naming the behavior
4. If they regress, temporarily bring points back -- that's normal, not failure

The typical timeline is 6 to 14 weeks for a single behavior: a few weeks of intensive use, a few weeks of gradual fading, and then praise alone carries it.

## Age-Specific Tips

**Toddlers (2-3):** Use a sticker board with 3 to 5 spots. Physical stickers they can place themselves. One behavior at a time. Reward within the hour.

**Preschool (3-5):** Marble jars work wonderfully. The child can see and hear progress. Reward by end of day. Two behaviors maximum.

**Elementary (5-10):** Star charts or simple point tracking. Three to five behaviors. Weekly rewards start to work. Let them help design the chart.

**Tweens (10-12):** Point systems with a "reward catalog" at different price levels. They can negotiate the menu. Self-monitoring (they track their own behavior) builds independence.

**Teens (13+):** Skip point systems entirely. Use level-based privileges instead. More responsibility unlocks more freedom. Co-design the agreement together -- they need genuine input or they'll ignore it.

## Common Mistakes

> **Try This (Avoid These Pitfalls)**
>
> **Reward too big, too soon.** Starting with a video game means you can't escalate. Start with extra story time and work up.
>
> **Too many behaviors at once.** A chart with 8 items overwhelms everyone. Start with 1-2, add more after those are solid.
>
> **Inconsistency between parents.** If Mom gives a point and Dad doesn't for the same thing, the whole system breaks down. Align before you start.
>
> **No fading plan.** If the system runs forever, it creates a kid who only performs for rewards. Set a mental timeline: "We'll reassess in 6 weeks."
>
> **Using it as punishment in disguise.** "I'm taking your points away" is a punishment, not a reward system. Keep it positive.

## Tracking It with OpenAuntie

Once your system is running, tell Auntie how each day went. She'll track streaks, spot patterns (does Max always lose the "brush teeth" point on weekdays?), and tell you when it might be time to fade.

> **You:** Max earned 2 out of 3 points today. He got dressed and cleared his plate but needed two reminders for teeth.
>
> **Auntie:** Logged! Max is at 85% this week overall, which is strong. The teeth brushing is the one that keeps slipping -- it's been the hardest one all week. Want me to suggest a tweak specifically for that one?

Small conversations, real progress.
