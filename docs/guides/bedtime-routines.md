# Setting Up Bedtime Routines

## Why Bedtime Matters Most

Of all the routines you could work on, bedtime has one of the best-supported evidence bases. Decades of research say the same thing: a consistent bedtime routine with 2 to 4 calming activities, done in the same order each night, lasting about 30 to 40 minutes, makes a real difference.

Research shows that a regular bedtime — going to bed at roughly the same time each night — is one of the strongest predictors of good emotional and behavioral regulation in children. Both consistency AND adequate sleep matter, but if you can only improve one thing, making bedtime predictable (within a 30-minute window) is one of the most impactful changes you can make.

This means you don't have to be perfect. You just have to be consistent.

---

## Designing the Routine

A good bedtime routine has a few key qualities:

- **2 to 4 activities** in the same order every night
- **30 to 40 minutes** from start to lights out
- **No screens** for the entire routine (screens suppress melatonin and wake the brain up)
- **Ends in the bedroom** where the child sleeps
- **Includes reading** (the single most beneficial bedtime activity for both sleep and learning)
- **Feels warm and connected** -- this is bonding time, not compliance time

**A good routine might look like:**
1. Bath or wash up (10 min)
2. Pajamas and brush teeth (5 min)
3. Read two books together (15 min)
4. Goodnight talk or gratitude ("What was one good thing about today?") (5 min)
5. Lights out with a hug

**Activities to avoid at bedtime:**
- TV, tablets, or phone time (blue light plus stimulating content)
- Sugary snacks or drinks
- Roughhousing or high-energy play
- Anything that winds them UP instead of DOWN

## Setting It Up with OpenAuntie

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** Create a bedtime routine for Emma. She's 4 and has been fighting bedtime lately.
>
> **Auntie:** Let's design one together. A few questions first:
>
> 1. What time do you want lights out?
> 2. Does Emma take a bath at night or in the morning?
> 3. What does she love? Stories, songs, stuffed animals?
> 4. Is anyone else involved in bedtime (partner, older sibling)?

> **You:** Lights out at 7:30. She does bath at night. She loves being read to and she has a stuffed bunny she sleeps with. David and I alternate nights.
>
> **Auntie:** Perfect. Here's a routine for Emma:
>
> **7:00** -- Bath time
> **7:10** -- Pajamas and brush teeth (let her pick the pajamas)
> **7:15** -- Two books in bed with Bunny
> **7:25** -- Goodnight talk: "What made you happy today?"
> **7:30** -- Hug, tuck in, lights out
>
> I've saved this so both you and David can follow the same steps. Consistency between both of you is really important -- Emma needs the routine to feel predictable no matter who's on bedtime duty.
>
> One tip: the first two weeks will be the hardest. Expect some pushback, especially since she's been fighting it. Stick with it -- it usually clicks after 2 to 3 weeks.

## Logging How It Went

After bedtime, give Auntie a quick update. It doesn't have to be detailed.

> **You:** Bedtime went okay tonight. Emma did the whole routine but cried for about 5 minutes after lights out.
>
> **Auntie:** Logged! Five minutes of crying after lights out is actually a big improvement from what you described last week. She completed all the steps, which is great. The crying should decrease as the routine becomes more predictable for her. Let's see how the rest of the week goes.

> **Try This**
>
> After bedtime each night, tell Auntie three things:
> 1. Which routine steps were completed
> 2. How much resistance there was (none, some, a lot)
> 3. Anything unusual (skipped bath, late start, visitors in the house)
>
> Even a one-sentence update helps Auntie track patterns.

## Reading the Trends

After a week or two of logging, Auntie can show you patterns you might not notice yourself.

> **You:** How has bedtime been going this week?
>
> **Auntie:** Here's what I'm seeing for Emma this week:
>
> - She completed all routine steps 5 out of 7 nights (up from 3 out of 7 last week)
> - The "brush teeth" step gets the most resistance -- it was skipped or fought on 3 nights
> - Nights when David does bedtime go slightly smoother (possibly because bath time is more playful?)
> - No more crying after lights out since Wednesday -- that's a 4-night streak!
>
> The teeth brushing is the weak link. Want to brainstorm a small tweak for just that step?

This kind of pattern detection is hard to do in your own head when you're exhausted at 8pm. That's what Auntie is for.

## Tips That Make a Difference

> **Try This**
>
> **Keep it within a 30-minute window.** Starting bedtime at 7:00 one night and 8:15 the next undermines the whole routine. Pick a time and protect it.
>
> **Build a wind-down sequence.** Start calming things down 15 to 20 minutes before the routine even begins. Dim the lights, lower your own voice, slow the pace of the house.
>
> **Let your child help design the routine.** Offer limited choices: "Do you want to brush teeth before or after pajamas?" Kids who have a say in the routine cooperate more.
>
> **Use the routine as the authority, not yourself.** Instead of "I'm telling you it's time for bed," try "The schedule says it's bath time now." This small shift reduces power struggles because you're not the bad guy -- the routine is just what happens next.
>
> **Expect regression around weeks 2 to 3.** Almost every child will test the new routine after the initial honeymoon period. This is normal. It does not mean the routine isn't working. Hold steady.
>
> **Include something your child looks forward to.** If the routine is all tasks and no joy, they'll resist it. The book, the goodnight talk, the special hug -- those are the anchors that make the whole thing work.

## When to Worry

Most bedtime struggles are normal, especially during transitions (new school, new sibling, dropped nap, developmental leap). Give a new routine 3 to 6 weeks before deciding it isn't working.

But talk to your child's doctor if:
- Your child snores loudly or stops breathing during sleep
- They have frequent night terrors (different from nightmares)
- They cannot fall asleep even with a consistent routine after 6 weeks
- Daytime sleepiness is affecting school or daily life

Auntie will flag these concerns if your logs suggest something beyond normal bedtime resistance.
