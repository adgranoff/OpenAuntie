# Trip Point Systems: The Vacation Tracker

## The Cruise Tracker Idea

This one started on a family cruise. Three kids, seven days, and a ship full of tempting activities -- the mini-golf, the rock wall, the ice cream bar, the arcade. Every hour was "Can we do this? Can we do that?"

The solution: each kid got 3 points per day. Activities cost points. Spend them however you want. When they're gone, they're gone. Tomorrow you get 3 more.

It transformed the trip. No more constant negotiating. No more "that's not fair" arguments. The kids made choices, managed their own fun, and learned something about budgeting along the way.

You can use this pattern for any trip or vacation -- not just cruises.

---

## How It Works

The system is simple:

1. **Each child gets a set number of points per day** (3 is the sweet spot for most families)
2. **Activities cost different amounts** based on expense, time, or effort
3. **Points reset daily** -- no rolling over to the next day
4. **Kids choose** how to spend their points from a menu of options
5. **When points are spent, they're spent** -- no borrowing, no extra

### Why Daily Reset?

No rollover keeps things simple. It prevents hoarding, eliminates "but I saved up" arguments, and gives every day a fresh start. A bad day of choices yesterday doesn't haunt you today.

## Setting Up a Trip Plan with OpenAuntie

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** Plan a spring break trip with 3 points per day. We're going to the beach for 5 days. Kids are Max (7) and Emma (4).
>
> **Auntie:** Fun! Let me help you build an activity menu. Here's a starting point -- adjust based on what's actually available:
>
> **Daily Points: 3 per child**
>
> **1-Point Activities:**
> - Ice cream or special snack from the boardwalk
> - Extra 30 minutes of screen time in the evening
> - Pick the restaurant for dinner
> - Choose the family activity for the afternoon
>
> **2-Point Activities:**
> - Rent boogie boards for the afternoon
> - Arcade visit ($5 budget)
> - Souvenir shop ($10 budget)
> - Mini-golf
>
> **3-Point Activities (full day's budget):**
> - Parasailing or boat tour
> - Water park visit
> - Go-karts
> - A "big" souvenir ($20 budget)
>
> A few things that make this work:
>
> 1. **Post the menu** where everyone can see it (print it, or put it on a phone everyone can check)
> 2. **Let kids plan their day each morning** over breakfast: "You have 3 points. What do you want to do today?"
> 3. **Include free activities too** so kids know there's always fun available even with zero points: beach time, swimming, building sandcastles, card games, exploring
> 4. **Respect their choices.** If Max wants 3 ice cream cones instead of mini-golf, that's his call. That's the system working.
>
> Want me to track points for each day?

## Making the Activity Menu

> **Try This**
>
> Before the trip, sit down with your kids and build the menu together. This is half the fun.
>
> 1. Write down every activity and treat that might be available on the trip.
> 2. Together, decide what's a 1-point, 2-point, or 3-point activity.
> 3. Include some things that are free (beach time, hiking, card games) -- these don't cost any points.
> 4. Print or write the menu on a card everyone can carry.
>
> When kids help build the menu, they understand the system better and feel ownership over it. They'll argue less about the prices because they helped set them.

## Daily Tracking

Each morning, start fresh with 3 points per child. Track them throughout the day.

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** Day 2 update. Max spent 2 points on boogie board rental and 1 point on ice cream. Emma spent 1 point on ice cream and 2 points at the arcade. Both had a great day.
>
> **Auntie:** Logged!
>
> **Day 2 Summary:**
> - Max: 3/3 points spent (boogie boards + ice cream). Zero remaining.
> - Emma: 3/3 points spent (ice cream + arcade). Zero remaining.
>
> Both kids are spending all 3 points each day, which is totally fine. That's what the points are for! If you notice one of them wanting to "save" a point but feeling pressure to spend, you could add a small bonus for unused points -- like a sticker on a trip chart. But only if it comes up naturally.
>
> Day 3 is a fresh start with 3 more points each. Any big activities you're thinking about for tomorrow?

## Adapting the System

### For Younger Kids (3-5)
Keep it even simpler. Use 2 points per day and only 1-point or 2-point activities. Use physical tokens (coins, pom-poms, stickers) they can hold and hand over when they "spend" one. The physical act of giving up a token makes the choice feel real.

### For Older Kids (10+)
Give them more agency. Let them manage their own budget entirely. They might even want a small amount of real money instead of points. The principle is the same: fixed daily budget, their choices, no bailouts.

### For Mixed Ages
Everyone gets the same number of points. The 4-year-old and the 12-year-old both get 3 points. This avoids "that's not fair" complaints. The activity menu can have different options that appeal to different ages, but the budget is equal.

## Beyond Vacations

The trip point system works for any special event or period:

**Holiday break at home:** 3 points per day. Activities: movie time, baking project, craft supplies, special outing, extra screen time.

**Summer weeks:** 3 points per day. Activities: pool time, friend playdates, ice cream runs, special activities.

**Theme park days:** 3-5 points for the day. Activities: rides, snacks, games, souvenirs.

**Weekends:** 2-3 points for Saturday. Activities: screen time blocks, special snacks, choosing the family activity.

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** We're doing a staycation this week. Can we set up a point system for activities at home?
>
> **Auntie:** Absolutely! Here's a home version:
>
> **Daily Points: 3 per child**
>
> **1 Point:** Choose a show to watch, pick the snack, 30 minutes of video games
>
> **2 Points:** Baking project with a parent, friend comes over to play, trip to the park with a special treat
>
> **3 Points:** Movie theater outing, swimming pool trip, choose-your-own dinner night
>
> **Free (no points needed):** Reading, outdoor play, board games, drawing, helping cook
>
> Want me to track it for the week?

## Why Kids Love This

The trip point system works because it gives kids real power. They're not asking permission for every treat -- they're making decisions within a clear structure. They learn that choosing one thing means not choosing another. They learn that special experiences have value. And they do it all without constant negotiation with you.

> **Try This**
>
> At the end of the trip, ask each child: "What was your favorite thing you spent points on? Is there anything you wish you'd done differently?" This reflection cements the budgeting lesson and helps them plan better next time.
>
> Some families make trip-point planning a tradition. The kids look forward to building the activity menu almost as much as the trip itself.

You might be surprised how quickly kids start thinking strategically: "If I skip ice cream today, I can do the boat tour tomorrow." That's budgeting. That's planning. And it happened because you gave them a system instead of a lecture.
