# Getting Started with OpenAuntie

## Your First 10 Minutes

### What Is OpenAuntie?

Think of that one aunt in the family who has seen it all. She raised her own kids, she watched yours grow up, and when you call her at 10pm because bedtime went sideways, she doesn't judge. She listens, she asks smart questions, and she gives you something you can actually try tonight.

That's OpenAuntie. Except this auntie also reads the research and gives you advice grounded in what actually works. With the full setup (Claude Desktop), she also keeps track of how things are going with your kids and never forgets what you told her last Tuesday.

OpenAuntie is not a therapist. She's not another parent on social media making you feel bad. She's a practical, warm, evidence-based helper who stores your family's tracking data on your own device. Your conversation messages are processed by the AI provider (like Claude or ChatGPT) — the same as any other AI chat.

### Quick Start Prompts — Copy and Paste These

Not sure what to say? Copy any of these into your chat with Auntie and fill in the blanks:

> "Help me set up my family profile. I have two kids — you can call them [nickname], age [age], and [nickname], age [age]. We're working on [whatever challenge]."
>
> **Tip:** Real names are completely optional. Nicknames, initials, or even made-up names work perfectly — Auntie just needs ages and context to give good advice.

> "My [AGE]-year-old has been [SPECIFIC BEHAVIOR]. What should I try?"

> "What should I expect from a [AGE]-year-old in terms of [TOPIC — e.g., homework, emotions, bedtime]?"

**These work with the full tracking setup (Claude Desktop):**

> "Set up a bedtime routine for [CHILD NAME] with these steps: bath, brush teeth, read a book, lights out."

> "How many points does [CHILD NAME] have?"

> "How was this week?"

### Setting Up Your Family Profile

The first thing to do is tell Auntie about your family. Just talk to her like you'd talk to a real person.

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** Help me set up my family profile. I have two kids — call them M, he's 7, and E, she's 4. My partner and I are both involved.
>
> **Auntie:** Welcome! I'm so glad you're here. Let me get to know your family a bit.
>
> So we have M at 7 and E at 4 — fun ages! Before we go further, can you tell me a little about each kid? What are their personalities like? What's going well, and what's been tricky lately?

> **You:** M is really energetic and creative, but getting him to do anything in the morning is like herding cats. E is pretty easygoing but she's started having huge meltdowns at bedtime.
>
> **Auntie:** Got it — energetic M who's tough to wrangle in the mornings, and E who's recently started bedtime battles. Both of those are really common at their ages, and both are things I can help with. *(With the full tracking setup, Auntie saves this to your family profile for future reference.)*
>
> Want to start with one of those, or is there something else on your mind?

*(This example uses initials, but real names work fine too — whatever you're comfortable sharing.)*

That's it. No forms to fill out. Just a conversation.

### Your First Question to Auntie

You can ask about anything. Here's what a real question looks like:

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** E has been screaming at bedtime for the past week. She used to go down fine. What's going on?
>
> **Auntie:** That sounds exhausting, especially when it's a change from what was working before. A few questions to help me help you:
>
> 1. Has anything changed recently -- new school, schedule shift, dropped a nap, new sibling conversations?
> 2. What does your bedtime routine look like right now?
> 3. When the screaming starts, what usually happens next?
>
> Age 4 is a really common time for bedtime regressions, even in kids who were great sleepers. It doesn't mean you did something wrong. Let me know the details and I'll give you something specific to try.

Auntie always asks before she advises. She wants to understand your situation, not give you generic tips.

### What You Can Track

OpenAuntie helps you keep track of the things that slip through the cracks:

- **Routines** -- Morning and bedtime routines with streak tracking. See which steps keep getting skipped.
- **Behavior and rewards** -- Point systems, chore charts, and the 5-to-1 positive ratio.
- **Emotions** -- Daily mood check-ins using color-coded zones (Blue, Green, Yellow, Red).
- **Health** -- Medication reminders, doctor appointments, growth records.
- **School** -- Reading logs, homework patterns, learning goals.
- **Money** -- The three-jar allowance system (Save, Spend, Give).
- **Family time** -- Trip planning, activity ideas, family meeting agendas.

You don't have to use all of these. Start with whatever feels most useful.

### What to Try in Your First Week

> **Try This**
>
> **Day 1:** Set up your family profile (just tell Auntie about your kids).
>
> **Day 2:** Ask one real question -- whatever is bugging you most right now.
>
> **Day 3:** Set up a bedtime or morning routine for one child.
>
> **Day 4:** Log how the routine went. Just tell Auntie: "Bedtime was rough tonight" or "Morning went great."
>
> **Day 5:** Ask Auntie to review the week so far. She'll spot patterns you might miss.
>
> **Day 6-7:** Try one thing Auntie suggested. Report back. She'll help you adjust.

The goal for week one is not to overhaul your family. It's to get comfortable talking to Auntie and see what she can do. Small steps, real results.

### Privacy

Your family's stored data (points, routines, health records) is saved as files on your device. OpenAuntie itself never uploads these files. With the **full tracking setup** (Claude Desktop), relevant data snippets may be included in conversation messages sent to the AI provider to give personalized advice. With the **ChatGPT-only setup**, only what you type yourself is sent to OpenAI. For maximum privacy, use nicknames and avoid sharing sensitive medical details.

When something comes up that's beyond what Auntie can help with -- signs of a developmental concern, serious mental health questions, safety issues -- she'll tell you honestly and point you to the right professional. That's what a good auntie does.
