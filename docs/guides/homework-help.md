# Homework Help That Actually Helps

## The Surprising Research

Here's something most parents don't know: **homework has no measurable academic benefit in elementary school.** None. This isn't one person's opinion -- it's the conclusion of the largest research review ever done on the topic, covering decades of studies.

For middle schoolers, the benefit is modest. Only in high school does homework start to clearly help, and even then it plateaus after about 2 hours per night.

This doesn't mean you should ignore homework or fight with the school about it. It means you can stop feeling guilty that homework time is a nightly battle in your house. For young kids especially, reading together, playing, and being curious matter far more than worksheets.

---

## The 10-Minute Rule

The National PTA and National Education Association recommend about 10 minutes of homework per grade level per night.

| Grade | Recommended maximum |
|-------|-------------------|
| Kindergarten | 0 minutes (reading and play ARE the homework) |
| 1st grade | 10 minutes |
| 3rd grade | 30 minutes |
| 6th grade | 60 minutes |
| 9th grade | 90 minutes |
| 12th grade | 120 minutes |

If your child is consistently spending much more than this, something needs attention. It could be that the assignments are too heavy (talk to the teacher), that your child has a skill gap (they need help, not more practice), or that something in the environment is making focus hard (distractions, anxiety, perfectionism).

---

## The Biggest Mistake Parents Make

It feels right to sit next to your child and help them through each problem. It's not.

Research shows that **controlling help** -- sitting next to them, correcting each answer, explaining the "right way" before they try -- actually leads to lower test scores, less motivation, and more homework anxiety. The parents who help the most often help the least. This is one of the most consistent findings in education research.

What works instead is **being available without hovering.**

> **Try This**
>
> **Do:** Set up a good homework environment (quiet spot, consistent time, materials ready), then leave the room. Be nearby in case they need you.
>
> **Do:** When they ask for help, respond with a question: "What do you think the first step is?" or "What part is tripping you up?"
>
> **Don't:** Sit next to them correcting each problem as they write it.
>
> **Don't:** Explain the answer before they've had a chance to struggle with it. Struggle is where learning happens.
>
> **Don't:** Take over when they're frustrated. Say "This is hard. What's one different approach you could try?" instead.

## Growth Mindset: What It Really Means

You've probably heard "praise effort, not smarts." There's more to it than that.

Growth mindset doesn't mean praising a child for trying hard when they're stuck and going nowhere. It means praising **strategy** -- when they try a different approach, when they learn from a mistake, when they ask for help in a smart way.

**Say this:**
- "You tried three different ways before you figured it out. That's what good learners do."
- "You're not there yet. What's one thing you could try differently?"
- "What did you learn from the part that didn't work?"

**Not this:**
- "You're so smart!" (ties their identity to being naturally gifted)
- "Great effort!" (when they've been spinning their wheels without progress)
- "Some people are just better at math." (teaches them ability is fixed)

The key insight: growth mindset is about trying **new strategies**, not just trying **harder**. A child who has been doing the same thing for 30 minutes without progress doesn't need encouragement to keep going. They need help finding a different path.

## Setting Up the Environment

A consistent homework routine removes the daily negotiation about when and where it happens.

> **Try This**
>
> Set up homework using these four elements:
>
> 1. **Consistent time.** Same time every day, linked to the daily routine ("homework happens after snack"). Not negotiated each day.
> 2. **Consistent place.** Same spot -- kitchen table, desk, wherever works. Materials already there.
> 3. **No distractions.** TV off. Phone in another room (yours too). Siblings doing their own thing.
> 4. **"Homework before privileges."** Screen time, video games, and playdates happen after homework is done. This isn't a punishment -- it's a sequence. "When homework is done, then screens." Same "when-then" language from routine building.

## Tracking Homework Struggles

When you tell Auntie about homework, she can spot patterns you might miss.

**Example conversation:**

> **You:** Max had a meltdown over math homework again tonight. It took an hour for 20 minutes of work.
>
> **Auntie:** I'm sorry -- that sounds really draining for both of you. This is the third math meltdown this week. A few things I'm noticing:
>
> 1. The meltdowns only happen with math, not reading or writing. That suggests something specific about math is hard right now.
> 2. They happen more on days when homework starts after 5pm. Max might be too tired by then.
> 3. Last week he had zero math meltdowns when you moved homework to right after snack at 3:30.
>
> The pattern suggests two things: the math might have a skill gap worth mentioning to his teacher, and earlier homework time makes a real difference for Max. What do you think?

> **You:** That makes sense. Should I be worried about the math thing?
>
> **Auntie:** Not yet -- it's worth a conversation with his teacher to find out if he's struggling with a specific concept. At 7, a short-term skill gap is very normal and very fixable. The meltdown is usually about frustration, not inability. If the pattern continues after the teacher gives some extra support, then we might want to look at whether something else is going on. But let's start with the teacher conversation.

## When to Worry vs. When It's Normal

**Normal:** Not wanting to do homework. Complaining. Needing occasional help. Having harder days and easier days.

**Worth watching:** Consistent avoidance of one subject. Homework taking dramatically longer than it should. Physical symptoms (stomachaches, headaches) before homework time. The same struggle repeating week after week despite support.

**Talk to the teacher if:** Your child is consistently spending much more time than the 10-minute rule suggests. The teacher can tell you whether the workload is appropriate and whether your child is struggling with specific skills.

**Talk to Auntie if:** You're not sure whether what you're seeing is normal. She can look at the patterns in your logs and help you figure out if this is a rough patch or something that needs attention.

---

## The Bottom Line

> **Try This**
>
> Shift your role from "homework supervisor" to "homework environment manager."
>
> Your job: set up the right time, right place, right sequence, and make yourself available.
>
> Your child's job: do the actual work.
>
> If they struggle, ask questions instead of giving answers. If they fail, that's data, not disaster. And if homework is a nightly war in elementary school, remember: the research says it's not even helping them academically at that age. Focus on reading together, being curious, and keeping the relationship intact. Those matter more.
