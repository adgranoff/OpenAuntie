# Collaborative & Proactive Solutions (CPS)
Last reviewed: 2026-03

This document covers Ross Greene's Collaborative & Proactive Solutions model (formerly Collaborative Problem Solving). CPS is an evidence-based approach to understanding and addressing challenging behavior in children by identifying lagging skills and unsolved problems rather than imposing consequences.

---

## Core Philosophy: "Kids Do Well If They Can"

The foundational premise of CPS is that challenging behavior occurs when the demands of the environment exceed a child's capacity to respond adaptively. This is a radical departure from the conventional assumption — "kids do well if they want to" — which frames misbehavior as a choice driven by insufficient motivation.

> Greene's central thesis is that children who exhibit challenging behavior are not unmotivated, manipulative, attention-seeking, or limit-testing. They are lacking the cognitive skills (flexibility, frustration tolerance, problem-solving) required to meet the expectations placed on them. When these skills are taught rather than demanded, challenging behavior decreases. [Greene, 2014, "The Explosive Child," revised edition, Harper] [MODERATE] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### Implications of "Kids Do Well If They Can"

| If you believe... | Then you respond with... | Result |
|-------------------|-------------------------|--------|
| Kids do well if they **want** to | Rewards and punishment to increase motivation | Short-term compliance, long-term escalation, damaged relationship |
| Kids do well if they **can** | Skill identification and collaborative problem-solving | Durable behavior change, skill development, preserved relationship |

> This reframing is supported by research in developmental psychopathology showing that the majority of chronically challenging children have identifiable skill deficits in domains such as executive function, language processing, emotional regulation, cognitive flexibility, and social thinking. [Greene & Ablon, 2006, "Treating Explosive Kids: The Collaborative Problem-Solving Approach," Guilford Press] [MODERATE] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

## The Three Plans

CPS organizes all adult responses to unmet expectations into three categories. Choosing the right Plan for the right problem is central to the model.

### Plan A: Impose Adult Will

The adult insists on their expectation being met, using whatever authority is necessary.

**When to use Plan A:**
- Genuine safety emergencies ("You cannot run into the street")
- Non-negotiable health requirements (car seats, medication)

**What Plan A looks like:**
- "You must do X because I said so."
- The adult's expectation is imposed without the child's input.

**The problem with overusing Plan A:**
> Greene's clinical research found that overuse of Plan A (imposing adult will on problems that are not genuine safety issues) is the primary driver of meltdowns, power struggles, and explosive behavior in challenging children. Plan A does not teach missing skills and reliably triggers challenging episodes in children who lack the flexibility and frustration tolerance to comply. [Greene, 2014, "The Explosive Child"] [MODERATE] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### Plan B: Collaborate (The Core of CPS)

The adult and child work together to find a solution that addresses both the adult's concern and the child's concern. This is the primary tool of CPS and should be the default for most recurring problems.

**When to use Plan B:**
- Any problem that recurs predictably
- Situations where the child's perspective needs to be understood
- When Plan A has failed repeatedly
- When the goal is to build the child's problem-solving skills

### Plan C: Drop the Expectation (For Now)

The adult strategically and temporarily removes an expectation that is not essential, to reduce the total demand load on the child.

**When to use Plan C:**
- When a child has too many unsolved problems at once (prioritize)
- For expectations that are not developmentally critical right now
- To create "wins" and reduce the child's overall stress load
- When the same problem keeps escalating and neither Plan A nor Plan B has worked

**What Plan C is NOT:**
- Not permissive parenting (it is a deliberate, temporary, strategic choice)
- Not giving up (the expectation can return when other problems are solved and skills have grown)
- Not letting the child "win" (it is removing a demand to focus on higher-priority demands)

> Greene emphasizes that most families try to address too many problems simultaneously, which overwhelms the child and fragments the parent's consistency. Plan C — strategically removing lower-priority expectations — is one of the most counterintuitive but effective elements of CPS. [Greene, 2014, "The Explosive Child"] [MODERATE] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### Choosing the Right Plan

| Question | Plan |
|----------|------|
| Is there an immediate safety risk? | **Plan A** |
| Is this a recurring, predictable problem? | **Plan B** |
| Is this important but NOT urgent, and the child already has too many problems? | **Plan C** |
| Has Plan A failed repeatedly for this problem? | Switch to **Plan B** |
| Has Plan B not yet been attempted? | Start with **Plan B** |

## Plan B: The Three Steps

Plan B is a structured conversation with three distinct phases. Each phase has a specific purpose, and skipping a phase undermines the process.

### Step 1: The Empathy Step

**Purpose**: Gather information about the child's concern, perspective, or difficulty regarding the unsolved problem.

**How to initiate** (Proactive Plan B — before the problem happens):
- "I've noticed that [describe the problem without blame]. What's up?"
- "I've noticed that getting ready for school in the morning has been really hard lately. What's going on?"

**Key skills for the Empathy Step:**
- **Drill down**: The first response is rarely the real concern. Follow up with: "What do you mean?" "Can you say more about that?" "How so?" "I'm not sure I understand yet."
- **Reflective listening**: Paraphrase what you hear: "So you're saying that..."
- **Avoid inserting your concern**: This step is exclusively about the child's perspective.
- **Don't problem-solve yet**: Resist the urge to jump to solutions.
- **Reassure safety**: "You're not in trouble. I'm trying to understand."

**Example:**
> Parent: "I've noticed that homework time has been really difficult lately. What's going on?"
> Child: "I hate homework."
> Parent: "You hate it. What is it about homework that you hate?" (drilling down)
> Child: "It takes forever."
> Parent: "It takes a long time. Can you say more about that?"
> Child: "By the time I get home from school I'm tired, and then I have to sit and do MORE school."
> Parent: "So after a long day at school, you're exhausted, and doing more academic work feels like too much." (reflective listening)
> Child: "Yeah. And then I don't have time to play."
> Parent: "Okay, so you're concerned that homework takes up your play time and you're too tired after school to concentrate. Did I get that right?"

### Step 2: Define the Problem

**Purpose**: Introduce the adult's concern so that both perspectives are on the table.

**Format**: "The thing is..." or "My concern is..."

**Key principles:**
- State your concern, not your solution. "My concern is that you're falling behind in math" NOT "My concern is that you need to do your homework right after school."
- Be specific and honest about why this matters to you.
- Keep it brief — one or two sentences.

**Example (continuing):**
> Parent: "I hear you. The thing is, I'm worried that if homework doesn't get done, your grades will drop and you'll fall behind in class, which would make things even harder."

### Step 3: The Invitation

**Purpose**: Invite the child to brainstorm a solution that addresses BOTH concerns.

**How to invite:**
- "I wonder if there's a way to [address child's concern] AND [address adult's concern]. Do you have any ideas?"

**Key principles:**
- The child generates solutions first (builds ownership and capability)
- Both concerns must be addressed — a solution that ignores the child's concern will fail
- The first solution may not be the best — keep brainstorming
- Test the solution: "Let's try that for a week and see how it goes"
- Solutions are agreements, not edicts — both parties commit

**Example (continuing):**
> Parent: "I wonder if there's a way for you to get some rest and play time after school AND make sure homework gets done so you don't fall behind. Do you have any ideas?"
> Child: "What if I play for an hour when I get home and THEN do homework?"
> Parent: "That could work. My worry is that sometimes an hour turns into two, and then it's dinner time. How could we make sure the homework still gets done?"
> Child: "I could set a timer for one hour, and when it goes off, I start homework."
> Parent: "Okay. So you'd play from 3:30 to 4:30, then start homework at 4:30 with a timer. Let's try it for a week and see how it goes. If it's not working, we'll put our heads together again."

## ALSUP: Assessment of Lagging Skills and Unsolved Problems

The ALSUP is a tool for identifying the specific skills a child is lacking and the specific situations (unsolved problems) where those skill deficits manifest. It is the diagnostic foundation of CPS.

> The ALSUP is available as a free resource from Lives in the Balance (livesinthebalance.org). It organizes lagging skills into categories and helps adults identify specific, predictable, recurring patterns rather than responding reactively to each individual incident. [Greene, Lives in the Balance; Greene & Ablon, 2006, Guilford Press] [MODERATE] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### Lagging Skills Categories

The ALSUP identifies skill deficits in these domains:

**Executive Function Skills:**
- Difficulty handling transitions, shifting from one mindset to another
- Difficulty doing things in a logical sequence or prescribed order
- Difficulty persisting on challenging or tedious tasks
- Difficulty considering the likely outcomes or consequences of actions (impulsivity)
- Difficulty considering a range of solutions to a problem

**Language Processing Skills:**
- Difficulty understanding what is being said
- Difficulty communicating needs, preferences, or concerns in words
- Difficulty managing emotional response to frustration so as to think rationally (separation of affect)

**Cognitive Flexibility Skills:**
- Difficulty handling unpredictability, ambiguity, uncertainty, novelty
- Difficulty shifting from original idea, plan, or solution
- Difficulty seeing the "gray" (concrete, literal, black-and-white thinking)
- Difficulty deviating from rules, routine

**Social Thinking Skills:**
- Difficulty attending to or accurately interpreting social cues
- Difficulty starting conversations, entering groups, connecting with people
- Difficulty appreciating how behavior is affecting other people
- Difficulty empathizing with others, appreciating another person's perspective

### Unsolved Problems

Unsolved problems are the specific, predictable, recurring situations where the child struggles. They are stated in terms of expectations:

- **Good format**: "Difficulty getting started on homework after school"
- **Good format**: "Difficulty staying seated during dinner"
- **Bad format**: "Defiant" (this is a label, not an unsolved problem)
- **Bad format**: "Needs to respect authority" (this is an adult solution, not a problem description)

### Using the ALSUP

1. **List unsolved problems**: Identify every recurring, predictable situation where the child struggles (typically 15-30 items)
2. **Identify lagging skills**: For each unsolved problem, identify which skill deficits contribute
3. **Prioritize**: Choose 1-3 unsolved problems to address first with Plan B (the rest go to Plan C temporarily)
4. **Start Plan B conversations**: One unsolved problem at a time

## Example Plan B Conversations

### Morning Routine Battles

**Empathy Step:**
> "I've noticed that getting out the door for school has been really stressful for both of us. What's making mornings so hard?"
> (Drill down until you understand: Is the child tired? Is there something anxiety-provoking at school? Do they dislike the sequence of tasks? Is the time pressure the problem?)

**Define the Problem:**
> "I hear you — mornings feel rushed and overwhelming. My concern is that when we leave late, you miss the bus and I'm late for work, which creates stress for the whole family."

**Invitation:**
> "I wonder if there's a way for mornings to feel less rushed AND get us all out the door on time. What ideas do you have?"

**Possible co-created solutions:**
- Prepare clothing and backpack the night before
- Wake up 15 minutes earlier with a preferred activity (reading, music) as incentive
- Use a visual checklist so the child manages their own sequence
- Shift the order of tasks (get dressed first, eat breakfast last — or vice versa)

### Homework Resistance

**Empathy Step:**
> "I've noticed that homework time often ends up with both of us frustrated. Help me understand what's making homework so difficult."
> (Drill down: Is it fatigue? Difficulty of the material? Too much homework? Feels pointless? Would rather do something else? Doesn't understand the instructions?)

**Define the Problem:**
> "The thing is, I'm concerned that not completing homework will affect your grades and your relationship with your teacher."

**Invitation:**
> "I wonder if there's a way to make homework less awful for you AND make sure it gets done. What do you think?"

### Sibling Conflict

**Empathy Step (with each child separately first):**
> "I've noticed that you and [sibling] have been getting into a lot of arguments about sharing the tablet. What's going on from your perspective?"
> (Each child gets a full Empathy Step before bringing them together)

**Define the Problem:**
> "So [Child A], you feel like [Child B] always takes more time. And [Child B], you feel like [Child A] always gets to go first. My concern is that the fighting is making everyone miserable and I end up taking the tablet away, which makes both of you unhappy."

**Invitation (with both children):**
> "I wonder if there's a way for both of you to get fair tablet time AND stop the fighting. Do you two have any ideas?"

## Research Evidence

### Recognition by the California Evidence-Based Clearinghouse (CEBC)

> Collaborative Problem Solving (now Collaborative & Proactive Solutions) is recognized by the California Evidence-Based Clearinghouse for Child Welfare (CEBC) as a supported intervention for children with behavioral challenges. [CEBC, Rating: 3 — Promising Research Evidence] [MODERATE] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### Clinical Outcomes

> Research conducted at Massachusetts General Hospital and Virginia Tech found that CPS produced outcomes equivalent to behavioral parent training approaches (reward/punishment systems) in reducing oppositional behavior, while showing superior outcomes in parent-child relationship quality. [Greene et al., 2004, "Effectiveness of Collaborative Problem Solving in Affectively Dysregulated Children," Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology] [MODERATE] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### Residential and School Settings

> Implementation of CPS in residential treatment facilities has been associated with reductions of 60-80% in restraint and seclusion episodes. In school settings, CPS implementation has been associated with significant reductions in discipline referrals, suspensions, and expulsions. [Greene, 2011, "Collaborative Problem Solving"; Pollastri et al., 2016, "Implementation of Collaborative Problem Solving in School Settings"] [MODERATE] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### Mechanism of Change

> The primary mechanism of change in CPS is not compliance but skill development. Children who participate in Plan B conversations develop improved frustration tolerance, flexibility, and problem-solving skills that transfer to situations beyond the specific unsolved problem addressed. The adult's empathic stance also reduces the adversarial dynamic that typically characterizes interactions between adults and challenging children. [Greene & Ablon, 2006, Guilford Press] [MODERATE] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

## When CPS May Not Be Sufficient

CPS is a powerful framework but is not a substitute for professional intervention when:

- The child's behavior includes serious safety concerns (self-harm, harm to others, fire-setting)
- The child has an undiagnosed or untreated neurodevelopmental condition (ADHD, autism, learning disability, anxiety disorder)
- The family system itself is destabilized (domestic violence, substance abuse, severe parental mental health issues)
- The child has experienced trauma that requires trauma-informed therapeutic intervention

In these cases, CPS can complement professional treatment but should not replace it.

## CPS vs. Other Approaches: When to Use What

| Situation | CPS (Plan B) | Positive Discipline (Consequences) | Behavior System (Rewards) |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Child lacks skills to meet expectation | First choice | May feel punitive | May not build underlying skill |
| Child has skill but environment makes it hard | Good fit | Good fit | Good fit |
| Chronic, recurring power struggles | First choice | Often fails (escalation cycle) | Often fails (low buy-in) |
| Building a new routine/habit | Can supplement | Good fit | Good fit for kickstart |
| Safety emergency | Plan A | Direct intervention | Not applicable |
| Many simultaneous problems | Essential (Plan C for prioritization) | Difficult to manage | Difficult to manage |
