# Evidence-Based Scripts
Last reviewed: 2026-03

This document provides "say this, not that" scripts organized by research framework and by common parenting situation. These scripts are the practical output of the evidence base documented in other knowledge files. OpenAuntie can draw on these scripts to give parents concrete, ready-to-use language.

---

## Gottman Emotion Coaching Scripts (5 Steps)

Based on John Gottman's emotion coaching research. See `emotion_coaching.md` for the full framework.

> Gottman's research with 120+ families found that emotion coaching parents — those who validate children's feelings and use emotional moments as teaching opportunities — raise children with better emotional regulation, academic performance, physical health, and peer relationships. The 5 steps below are the practical application of this research. [Gottman, Katz & Hooven, 1996, 1997, "Meta-Emotion: How Families Communicate Emotionally," Lawrence Erlbaum Associates] [STRONG] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### Step 1: Be Aware of the Emotion
- Notice before the child escalates: body language, tone shifts, withdrawal
- Internal check: "What is my child feeling right now?"

### Step 2: Recognize It as an Opportunity
- **Instead of**: "Here we go again."
- **Say to yourself**: "This is a chance to teach and connect."

### Step 3: Listen Empathetically and Validate
- **Say**: "I can see you're really upset about this."
- **Say**: "It sounds like that was really frustrating for you."
- **Say**: "Tell me more about what happened."
- **Do not say**: "It's not a big deal." / "You're fine." / "Stop crying."

### Step 4: Help Label the Emotion
- **Say**: "That sounds like disappointment."
- **Say**: "I wonder if you're feeling left out."
- **Say**: "It seems like you're angry AND sad at the same time. That can happen."
- **Do not say**: "You shouldn't feel that way." / "There's nothing to be scared of."

### Step 5: Set Limits and Problem-Solve
- **Say**: "It's okay to feel angry. It's not okay to hit. What could you do instead when you feel that angry?"
- **Say**: "Your feelings make sense. Now let's figure out what to do about it."
- **Do not say**: "I don't care how you feel, you can't act like that."

## ELC Method: Empathy-Limit-Choice

The ELC method is a three-step boundary-setting framework that combines emotional validation with firm limits and autonomy.

> The Empathy-Limit-Choice approach draws from Adlerian psychology (Dreikurs), Positive Discipline (Nelsen), and Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan). By validating the emotion (empathy), stating the boundary (limit), and offering agency (choice), parents address the child's emotional need, maintain the boundary, and reduce power struggles. [Nelsen, 2006, "Positive Discipline"; Deci & Ryan, 2000, "Self-Determination Theory"] [MODERATE] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### The Formula

**"I see [empathy]. The rule is [limit]. Do you want [choice A] or [choice B]?"**

### Scripts by Situation

| Situation | Script |
|-----------|--------|
| **Bedtime resistance** | "I see you want to keep playing. The rule is bedtime is at 8. Do you want to brush teeth first or put on pajamas first?" |
| **Leaving the playground** | "I know it's hard to leave when you're having fun. It's time to go. Do you want to go down the slide one more time or do one more swing?" |
| **Refusing to get dressed** | "I can see you don't want to stop playing. We need to get dressed for school. Do you want to pick your shirt or your pants first?" |
| **Not wanting to share** | "I understand you love that toy. When friends visit, we share our toys. Do you want to choose which toys to share and which to put away before they come?" |
| **Refusing vegetables** | "I get it — broccoli isn't your favorite. The rule is we try one bite of everything. Do you want to try it with ranch or with cheese?" |
| **Screen time ending** | "I know it's hard to stop when you're in the middle. Screen time is over. Do you want to turn it off yourself or should I?" |

## CPS Plan B Scripts (Collaborative & Proactive Solutions)

Based on Ross Greene's Collaborative & Proactive Solutions model. See `collaborative_problem_solving.md` for the full framework.

> Greene's research demonstrates that children who display challenging behavior are lacking the skills to meet expectations, not the will. Plan B (the collaborative approach) outperforms both Plan A (imposing adult will) and Plan C (dropping expectations) in durably solving recurring problems. [Greene, 2014, "The Explosive Child," revised edition; Greene, 2016, "Lost and Found"; Pollastri et al., 2013, Harvard Review of Psychiatry] [STRONG] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### The Three Steps of Plan B

**Step 1: Empathy Step** — Gather information about the child's concern
- **Say**: "I've noticed that [describe the unsolved problem]. What's up?"
- **Say**: "Help me understand what makes [situation] hard for you."
- **Say**: "What's going on with [situation] from your perspective?"
- **Do not say**: "Why can't you just...?" / "You need to..." / "Because I said so."
- **Key**: Use drilling questions. "What do you mean?" "Can you say more about that?" "I want to make sure I understand."

**Step 2: Define the Adult Concern** — Share your concern without imposing a solution
- **Say**: "The thing is, I'm worried that [adult concern]."
- **Say**: "My concern is [specific concern], because [reason]."
- **Example**: "My concern is that when homework waits until 9 PM, you're too tired to do it well and then you're upset about your grades."
- **Do not say**: "You have to do it my way." / "The rule is..." (Plan B is not about imposing rules)

**Step 3: Invitation** — Brainstorm solutions collaboratively
- **Say**: "I wonder if there's a way to [address child's concern] and also [address adult concern]. Do you have any ideas?"
- **Say**: "Let's figure this out together so it works for both of us."
- **Say**: "What do you think we could try?"
- **Key**: The solution must address BOTH concerns. If it only addresses one, return to brainstorming.

### Plan B Example: Homework Resistance

1. **Empathy**: "I've noticed homework has been really hard lately. You seem frustrated when it's time to start. What's going on?"
   - Child: "It's boring and I don't want to do it right after school."
   - Parent: "So you need a break after school before you can focus. That makes sense."
2. **Define concern**: "My concern is that when homework waits too long, it gets rushed at bedtime and then you're stressed."
3. **Invitation**: "So we need to find a time that gives you a break but isn't too late. Any ideas?"
   - Collaborate until you find a solution that addresses BOTH the child's need for a break AND the parent's concern about timing.

## Validation Scripts

Validation is the act of communicating that a person's emotional experience makes sense and is acceptable. It is not agreement, approval, or fixing.

> Research on emotional validation in parent-child relationships shows that children who receive consistent emotional validation develop stronger emotion regulation skills, higher self-esteem, and more secure attachment. Validation is the critical first step before any problem-solving or limit-setting. [Linehan, 1993, "Cognitive-Behavioral Treatment of Borderline Personality Disorder" (validation framework); Gottman, 1997, "Raising an Emotionally Intelligent Child"] [STRONG] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### Core Validation Phrases

| Validation Script | When to Use |
|-------------------|-------------|
| "I can see you're really frustrated." | Child is visibly upset |
| "It makes sense you'd feel that way." | Child's emotion is a reasonable response to the situation |
| "That sounds really hard." | Child is describing a difficult experience |
| "You're allowed to feel upset about this." | Child seems ashamed of their emotion |
| "I'd be disappointed too." | Normalizing the emotion by relating |
| "Of course you're sad. You lost something important to you." | Connecting the emotion to the cause |
| "Both things can be true — you can be excited about the trip AND sad about missing your friend's party." | Child has conflicting emotions |
| "I hear you." | Simple acknowledgment when child needs to feel heard |

### Validation Anti-Patterns (Do Not Say)

| What NOT to Say | Why It Fails |
|-----------------|-------------|
| "You're fine." / "You're okay." | Dismisses the child's emotional reality |
| "It's not that bad." / "It's not a big deal." | Minimizes the experience |
| "Other kids have it worse." | Invalidates through comparison |
| "Stop crying." / "Big kids don't cry." | Shames the emotional expression |
| "There's nothing to be afraid of." | Dismisses fear without addressing it |
| "You shouldn't feel that way." | Tells child their emotions are wrong |
| "Just calm down." | Demands regulation without supporting it |

## Cooperation Scripts (Faber & Mazlish)

Based on Faber & Mazlish's "How to Talk So Kids Will Listen & Listen So Kids Will Talk."

> Faber and Mazlish's communication approach, grounded in Haim Ginott's work, provides practical language alternatives that engage cooperation without threats, lectures, or commands. These techniques reduce power struggles by addressing the situation rather than attacking the child's character. [Faber & Mazlish, 1980/2012, "How to Talk So Kids Will Listen"; Ginott, 1965, "Between Parent and Child"] [MODERATE] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### Technique 1: Describe the Problem (Instead of Blaming)
- **Instead of**: "You left your wet towel on the bed AGAIN!"
- **Say**: "I see a wet towel on the bed."
- **Instead of**: "Why didn't you close the gate? The dog could have gotten out!"
- **Say**: "The gate is open and the dog is near the street."

### Technique 2: Give Information (Instead of Commands)
- **Instead of**: "Put the milk away RIGHT NOW!"
- **Say**: "Milk turns sour when it's left out."
- **Instead of**: "Wash your hands!"
- **Say**: "Hands need to be washed before we eat."

### Technique 3: Offer Choices (Instead of Ultimatums)
- **Instead of**: "Get in the bath or no story tonight."
- **Say**: "Do you want a bubble bath or a regular bath tonight?"
- **Instead of**: "Clean your room right now."
- **Say**: "Do you want to start with the books or the toys?"

### Technique 4: Say It with a Word (Instead of a Lecture)
- **Instead of**: "How many times have I told you to put your shoes on? We're going to be late. Every single morning it's the same thing..."
- **Say**: "Shoes!"
- **Instead of**: "You need to remember to hang up your backpack when you get home..."
- **Say**: "Backpack!"

### Technique 5: Describe How You Feel (Instead of Attacking Character)
- **Instead of**: "You're so inconsiderate!"
- **Say**: "I feel frustrated when I find the kitchen messy after I just cleaned it."
- **Instead of**: "You never listen!"
- **Say**: "I don't like having to repeat myself. It makes me feel ignored."

### Technique 6: Write a Note (Instead of Nagging)
- Post a note on the bathroom mirror: "Please return me to my hook! — The Towel"
- Post a note on the door: "Checklist: Shoes? Backpack? Lunch? Jacket?"
- Notes depersonalize reminders and can add humor

## Praise Scripts: Process vs. Person

> Carol Dweck's mindset research demonstrated that process praise ("You tried three different strategies!") builds a growth mindset and resilience, while person praise ("You're so smart!") builds a fixed mindset and fragility. Children praised for effort embrace challenges; children praised for ability avoid them. [Dweck, 2006, "Mindset: The New Psychology of Success"; Mueller & Dweck, 1998, Journal of Personality and Social Psychology] [STRONG] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### Process Praise (Use This)

| Situation | Process Praise Script |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Child gets a good grade | "You studied really hard for that test. Your effort paid off." |
| Child finishes a puzzle | "You tried three different strategies before you found one that worked. That's persistence." |
| Child draws a picture | "I notice you mixed those colors yourself. Tell me about what you made." |
| Child scores in a game | "All that practice is showing. You're really improving." |
| Child helps a sibling | "You noticed your sister was struggling and you helped her. That was kind." |
| Child fails but tries | "That didn't work out the way you wanted, but you kept trying. What would you do differently next time?" |

### Person Praise (Avoid This)

| Person Praise (Avoid) | Why It's Problematic | Process Alternative |
|----------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| "You're so smart!" | Creates fear of looking "not smart" | "You figured that out by trying different approaches." |
| "You're the best!" | Creates performance pressure | "You put a lot of effort into that." |
| "Good boy/girl!" | Ties worth to compliance | "That was a helpful thing to do." |
| "You're a natural!" | Attributes success to innate talent, not effort | "You've gotten so much better since you started practicing." |
| "I'm so proud of you!" | Centers adult's evaluation | "You must feel proud of yourself for that." |

## De-Escalation Scripts (When Parent or Child Is Activated)

### Parent Self-Regulation

When the parent feels themselves escalating — voice rising, body tensing, urge to yell or punish:

- **Say to yourself**: "I'm activated right now. I need to pause before I respond."
- **Say aloud**: "I'm going to take a breath before I respond." (models regulation)
- **Say aloud**: "I need a minute. I'll be right back." (take a brief time-out)
- **Say aloud**: "I love you, and I'm too upset to talk about this well right now. Let's come back to it in 10 minutes."

### Child De-Escalation

When the child is escalating but not yet in full meltdown:

- **Lower your voice** (whisper or speak very quietly — escalation follows volume)
- **Slow your breathing** visibly (the child's nervous system will attune)
- **Get physically low** (kneel, sit on the floor — remove the looming quality)
- **Say**: "I'm right here. You're safe."
- **Say**: "I can see this is really hard for you."
- **Say**: "Let's take some breaths together."
- **Do not say**: "Calm down." / "Stop it." / "You're being ridiculous."

### During Full Meltdown (Co-Regulate, Do Not Teach)

- Minimize words. The child's prefrontal cortex is offline.
- Offer physical comfort if the child will accept it: "Would a hug help?"
- Wait. Stay present. Stay calm. This will pass.
- **After the storm**: "That was a big feeling. I'm glad you're feeling calmer now. Want to talk about it?"

## Repair Scripts (After Parental Misstep)

All parents yell, snap, lose patience, and respond poorly. The repair is what matters.

> Research on attachment demonstrates that secure attachment does not require perfect parenting. It requires "good enough" parenting characterized by consistent repair after rupture. Children develop security not because their parents never err but because their parents reliably return, acknowledge the misstep, and reconnect. [Tronick, 2007, "The Neurobehavioral and Social-Emotional Development of Infants and Children"; Winnicott, 1953, "Transitional Objects and Transitional Phenomena"] [STRONG] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### The Repair Formula

**Acknowledge + Take Responsibility + Apologize + Recommit**

### Repair Scripts by Situation

| Situation | Repair Script |
|-----------|---------------|
| **You yelled** | "I raised my voice and that wasn't fair to you. I'm sorry. What I should have said is [calmer version]. Next time I feel that frustrated, I'm going to take a breath first." |
| **You said something hurtful** | "What I said about you being lazy was wrong. I didn't mean it. I was frustrated about the mess, but that's not an excuse to say hurtful things. I'm sorry." |
| **You were too harsh with a consequence** | "Taking your toy away for a whole week was too much. I was angry and went overboard. Let's figure out a consequence that's more fair." |
| **You dismissed their feelings** | "When you told me you were scared and I said 'don't be silly,' that wasn't kind. Your feelings matter. Tell me what scared you — I'm listening now." |
| **You weren't present** | "I was on my phone when you were trying to show me your drawing. That wasn't fair. Can you show me again? I'm all yours now." |
| **You snapped under stress** | "I snapped at you and that wasn't about you at all. I was stressed about work. You didn't deserve that. I'm sorry." |

### What NOT to Do in Repair

- **Do not add "but"**: "I'm sorry I yelled, BUT you were being so difficult" — this erases the apology
- **Do not over-apologize or grovel**: This makes the child responsible for the parent's emotions
- **Do not expect immediate forgiveness**: The child may need time. Allow it.
- **Do not use repair as an excuse to keep repeating the behavior**: If you're repairing the same thing weekly, the pattern needs to change

## Scripts by Common Situation

### Bedtime Resistance

| Approach | Script |
|----------|--------|
| ELC | "I see you want to keep playing. The rule is bedtime is at 8. Do you want to read a book or listen to a story?" |
| Cooperation | "Pajamas! Teeth! Books!" (word technique) |
| Validation + Limit | "I know it's hard to stop playing. It IS fun. And your body needs sleep to grow. Let's go." |
| Choice | "Do you want to hop to your room like a bunny or tiptoe like a ninja?" |
| Information | "Kids' brains grow while they sleep. That's why bedtime is important." |

### Homework Battles

| Approach | Script |
|----------|--------|
| CPS Plan B | "I've noticed homework time has been really stressful lately. What's making it hard?" |
| Choice | "Do you want to do homework at the kitchen table or at your desk?" |
| Information | "Your teacher says this is due Thursday. When do you want to plan to work on it?" |
| Validation | "I know this feels boring. Some things we have to do even when they're not fun." |
| Break-it-down | "Let's look at what you have. Which assignment feels smallest? Start there." |

### Sibling Fighting

| Approach | Script |
|----------|--------|
| Describe problem | "I see two kids who both want the same toy." |
| Sportscaster | "So you had it first, and you want a turn. That sounds frustrating for both of you." |
| Problem-solve | "What's a solution that works for both of you?" |
| Set limit | "It's okay to be angry at your brother. It's not okay to hit. Use your words." |
| Give space | "It sounds like you two need some time apart right now. [Name], you're in this room. [Name], you're in that room. When you're both calm, we'll figure it out." |

### Morning Chaos

| Approach | Script |
|----------|--------|
| Visual checklist | Post a morning routine chart: "Here's your checklist. Tell me when you've checked everything off." |
| Word technique | "Shoes! Backpack! Door!" |
| Choice | "Do you want to get dressed first or eat first?" |
| Timer | "Let's see if we can beat yesterday's time. Ready?" (gamification for younger kids) |
| Information | "The bus comes at 7:45. Right now it's 7:30. What do you still need to do?" |

### Screen Time Negotiation

| Approach | Script |
|----------|--------|
| ELC | "I know you want more time. The rule is 30 minutes on school days. Do you want to use it now or save it for after homework?" |
| Pre-agreement | "Before you start, let's agree: when the timer goes off, you'll turn it off without arguing. Deal?" |
| Validation + Limit | "I get it — it's hard to stop in the middle of a game. The rule doesn't change, but I understand your frustration." |
| Natural consequence | "If turning it off is a battle, we'll need to reduce screen time tomorrow so there's less to give up. If you can turn it off calmly, we keep the same amount." |
| CPS | "Screen time endings have been tough lately. What makes it so hard to stop?" |

### Public Meltdowns

| Approach | Script |
|----------|--------|
| Stay calm | "I'm right here. Let's take some breaths." (Ignore onlookers entirely.) |
| Reduce stimulation | "Let's go somewhere quieter." (Move to the car, a bathroom, a quiet corner.) |
| Validate | "I can see you're really upset. This is too much right now." |
| Do not lecture | Save all teaching for later. During the meltdown, the ONLY job is co-regulation. |
| After | "That was a really big feeling. You're feeling calmer now. Let's talk about what happened when we get home." |
| For onlookers (internal) | Remind yourself: every parent has been here. Your child is not "bad." You are not a "bad parent." You are a human with a small human who is having a hard time. |
