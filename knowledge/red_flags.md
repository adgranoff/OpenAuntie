# Developmental Red Flags
Last reviewed: 2026-03

This document provides age-based developmental red flags that signal when OpenAuntie should recommend professional evaluation. The agent does NOT diagnose. It identifies patterns that warrant a conversation with the child's pediatrician, developmental pediatrician, or specialist.

**OpenAuntie does NOT diagnose. If any of these patterns describe your child, please consult your pediatrician.**

---

## Universal Red Flag: Regression

> Loss of previously acquired skills (regression) at ANY age is a red flag that warrants immediate professional evaluation. Regression — losing words a child previously used, losing motor skills, losing social engagement — can indicate neurological, developmental, or medical conditions that require prompt assessment. Regression is never "just a phase." [AAP, 2020, "Identifying Infants and Young Children With Developmental Disorders in the Medical Home," Pediatrics; CDC Milestone Tracker Program, 2022] [CONSENSUS] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

Examples of regression:
- A child who was using 20 words stops speaking
- A child who was toilet trained begins having regular accidents (without obvious cause like new sibling or move)
- A child who was walking steadily begins falling frequently
- A child who was socially engaged withdraws from peers and adults
- A teenager who was functioning well academically and socially shows marked deterioration

**Action**: Always recommend immediate pediatric evaluation when regression is reported. Do not reassure. Do not suggest waiting.

## Birth to 2 Years: Monthly Milestones

Infant and toddler development is rapid, and delays in this period can signal conditions that benefit from early intervention. Earlier identification leads to better outcomes.

> The AAP recommends developmental screening at 9, 18, and 30 months, with autism-specific screening at 18 and 24 months. However, concerns at any age should prompt evaluation — parents do not need to wait for a scheduled screening. Research consistently shows that parents' concerns about their child's development are accurate predictors of actual developmental delays. [AAP, 2006/2020, "Identifying Infants and Young Children With Developmental Disorders in the Medical Home," Pediatrics] [CONSENSUS] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

## Ages 3-5: Preschool Red Flags

### Speech and Language

| Age | Red Flag |
|-----|----------|
| **3 years** | Speech is not understandable to unfamiliar adults at least 50% of the time |
| **3 years** | Does not speak in sentences of 3+ words |
| **3 years** | Does not understand simple instructions ("put the cup on the table") |
| **4 years** | Speech is not understandable to unfamiliar adults at least 75% of the time |
| **4 years** | Cannot tell a simple story or describe recent events |
| **4 years** | Does not ask "why" questions |
| **5 years** | Speech is not understandable to unfamiliar adults nearly 100% of the time |
| **5 years** | Cannot follow 3-step instructions |
| **5 years** | Does not use future tense ("I will go") |

### Motor Development

| Age | Red Flag |
|-----|----------|
| **3 years** | Falls frequently or has major difficulty with stairs |
| **3 years** | Cannot build a tower of more than 4 blocks |
| **3 years** | Has difficulty manipulating small objects |
| **4 years** | Cannot hop on one foot |
| **4 years** | Has difficulty scribbling or cannot copy a circle |
| **4 years** | Shows no interest in interactive games or make-believe |
| **5 years** | Cannot draw simple shapes (circle, square) |
| **5 years** | Cannot dress and undress without significant help |
| **5 years** | Cannot use fork and spoon with reasonable skill |

### Social and Emotional

| Age | Red Flag |
|-----|----------|
| **3 years** | Does not engage in pretend or make-believe play |
| **3 years** | Does not play with other children (not just alongside) by age 3.5 |
| **3 years** | Does not show a wide range of emotions |
| **3 years** | Extreme difficulty separating from parent (beyond typical separation anxiety) |
| **4 years** | Has no interest in playing with other children |
| **4 years** | Does not engage in fantasy play |
| **4 years** | Resists dressing, sleeping, using the toilet (with persistent intensity) |
| **4 years** | Cannot retell a favorite story |
| **5 years** | Does not show a range of emotions |
| **5 years** | Is unusually withdrawn or passive |
| **5 years** | Is unusually aggressive or fearful |
| **5 years** | Cannot distinguish real from make-believe |

> The AAP emphasizes that preschool-age children should be actively engaged in pretend play, social play with peers, and increasingly sophisticated language. Absence of pretend play by age 3, lack of peer interest by age 4, and persistent unintelligible speech beyond age 4 are significant red flags that warrant evaluation. [AAP Bright Futures Guidelines, 4th Edition, 2017; HealthyChildren.org developmental milestones] [CONSENSUS] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

## Ages 6-12: School-Age Red Flags

School-age red flags often emerge in academic, social, and emotional domains as developmental expectations increase.

### Speech and Language

| Red Flag | Concern |
|----------|---------|
| Stuttering that persists beyond age 6 or worsens | Fluency disorder requiring evaluation by speech-language pathologist |
| Difficulty finding the right words; frequent "um" and circumlocution | Word retrieval difficulties; possible language processing issue |
| Difficulty understanding age-appropriate jokes, sarcasm, or idioms | Pragmatic language concerns |
| Avoids reading aloud or reading tasks | Possible reading disability (dyslexia) |

> Stuttering that begins in the preschool years (ages 2-5) is common and often resolves spontaneously. However, stuttering that persists beyond age 6, worsens over time, or is accompanied by physical tension, avoidance of speaking situations, or emotional distress should be evaluated by a speech-language pathologist. Approximately 1% of the population has a persistent stuttering disorder. [ASHA, American Speech-Language-Hearing Association; Yairi & Ambrose, 2013, "Epidemiology of Stuttering: 21st Century Advances," Journal of Fluency Disorders] [STRONG] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### Academic and Cognitive

| Red Flag | Concern |
|----------|---------|
| Persistent difficulty following multi-step instructions despite understanding individual words | Processing speed or working memory concern |
| Significant gap between apparent intelligence and academic performance | Possible learning disability |
| Difficulty organizing tasks, materials, or time (beyond typical for age) | Executive function concern; possible ADHD |
| Academic regression — decline in grades or skills that were previously mastered | Emotional, neurological, or environmental issue |
| Avoidance or intense distress about specific academic tasks (reading, writing, math) | Possible learning disability in that domain |
| Extreme difficulty with handwriting despite adequate instruction | Possible dysgraphia |
| Persistent difficulty with basic math concepts despite instruction | Possible dyscalculia |

### Social

| Red Flag | Concern |
|----------|---------|
| Cannot make friends or has no reciprocal friendships | Social skill deficit; possible social communication disorder |
| Cannot keep friends (makes them but they don't last) | May indicate difficulty with social reciprocity, perspective-taking, or emotional regulation |
| Is consistently rejected or bullied by peers | May need social skills intervention; assess for contributing factors |
| Shows no interest in peers | Social communication concern; consider autism spectrum evaluation |
| Cannot read basic social cues (facial expressions, body language, tone of voice) | Pragmatic language or social communication concern |
| Extremely rigid about rules, routines, or fairness beyond what peers tolerate | May indicate anxiety, autism spectrum traits, or OCD |

### Emotional and Behavioral

| Red Flag | Concern |
|----------|---------|
| Emotional regulation significantly worse than peers (frequent meltdowns, rages, or shutdowns) | May indicate anxiety, ADHD, sensory processing difficulties, or trauma |
| Persistent anxiety that interferes with daily functioning (school refusal, sleep disruption, physical symptoms) | Anxiety disorder requiring professional evaluation |
| Persistent sadness, withdrawal, or loss of interest lasting more than 2 weeks | Possible depression; evaluate immediately |
| Extreme behaviors: cruelty to animals, fire-setting, persistent lying or stealing | Conduct concerns requiring professional evaluation |
| Self-harm (cutting, hitting self, head-banging in older children) | Immediate professional referral |
| Persistent sleep problems (difficulty falling asleep, nightmares, bedwetting) beyond age 7 | Medical and psychological evaluation |
| Sudden behavioral change (personality shift, withdrawal, aggression, fearfulness) | May indicate trauma, abuse, bullying, or medical condition |

> The AAP recommends that behavioral or emotional changes persisting more than 2 weeks — particularly depressive symptoms, anxiety that interferes with daily functioning, or significant behavioral regression — should be evaluated by a healthcare professional. Early intervention for childhood mental health conditions improves outcomes significantly. [AAP, 2018, "Mental Health Competencies for Pediatric Practice," Pediatrics; HealthyChildren.org] [CONSENSUS] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

## Speech and Language Red Flags: Summary Table by Age

| Age | Receptive Language (Understanding) | Expressive Language (Speaking) | Social Communication |
|-----|-----------------------------------|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| **9 months** | Does not respond to name | No babbling | No back-and-forth games |
| **12 months** | Does not follow simple gestures (point, wave) | No single words | No pointing or showing |
| **15 months** | Does not respond to "no" | Fewer than 3 words | Does not point to request or share |
| **18 months** | Cannot point to body parts when asked | Fewer than 6 words | Does not imitate words |
| **24 months** | Cannot follow simple 2-step instructions | Fewer than 50 words; no 2-word phrases | Does not engage in pretend play |
| **3 years** | Cannot understand simple stories | Speech < 50% intelligible to strangers; no 3-word sentences | No interest in other children |
| **4 years** | Cannot answer "who/what/where" questions | Speech < 75% intelligible; cannot tell a simple story | Cannot take conversational turns |
| **5 years** | Cannot follow 3-step instructions | Speech not fully intelligible; limited grammar | Difficulty with peer conversation |
| **6-7 years** | Difficulty understanding age-appropriate directions | Persistent articulation errors on common sounds; stuttering persists | Difficulty reading social situations |
| **8-12 years** | Difficulty with complex or ambiguous language | Word-finding problems; avoids speaking tasks | Difficulty with sarcasm, humor, idioms |

> Speech and language milestones are among the most reliable early indicators of developmental trajectories. Delays in language development are associated with later academic difficulties, social challenges, and behavioral problems. The AAP and ASHA recommend that any parent concern about speech and language development should prompt a referral for evaluation — the "wait and see" approach is no longer recommended. [AAP, 2020; ASHA Position Statements; CDC "Learn the Signs. Act Early," 2022] [CONSENSUS] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

## OpenAuntie Decision Framework

### Always Refer Immediately

These situations require an immediate recommendation to consult a professional:

| Situation | Referral Type |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Regression** — loss of any previously acquired skill at any age | Pediatrician immediately |
| **Suspected delay** — child is significantly behind age-matched milestones | Pediatrician or developmental pediatrician |
| **Safety concern** — self-harm, cruelty to animals, fire-setting, suicidal ideation | Mental health professional immediately; if acute danger, emergency services |
| **Sudden behavioral change** — personality shift, new fearfulness, withdrawal | Pediatrician to rule out medical and trauma |
| **Physical symptoms without medical explanation** — persistent headaches, stomach aches, sleep disruption | Pediatrician first, then mental health if medical causes ruled out |

### Gentle Referral

These situations warrant a suggestion to explore professional evaluation:

| Situation | Referral Type |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Persistent patterns** — behavior that has not improved after consistent intervention for 4+ weeks | Pediatrician or child psychologist |
| **Learning difficulties** — academic performance consistently below expected level | Psychoeducational evaluation (school or private) |
| **Anxiety or depression > 2 weeks** — worry, sadness, withdrawal, or somatic symptoms persisting | Child therapist or pediatrician |
| **Social difficulties** — persistent trouble making or keeping friends | Child psychologist; possible social communication evaluation |
| **Speech concerns** — any concern about speech clarity, language use, or communication | Speech-language pathologist |
| **Motor concerns** — persistent clumsiness, difficulty with fine motor tasks, or motor regression | Occupational therapist or pediatrician |

### How OpenAuntie Should Frame Red Flag Conversations

1. **Do not alarm unnecessarily**: "This may be nothing, but it's worth having checked."
2. **Do not diagnose**: "I'm not able to diagnose, and these patterns can have many explanations."
3. **Affirm the parent's instinct**: "You know your child best. If something feels off, trust that feeling."
4. **Normalize evaluation**: "Getting an evaluation doesn't mean something is wrong — it means you're making sure your child gets what they need."
5. **Reduce barriers**: "You can start by mentioning this to your pediatrician at the next visit, or call to schedule a developmental check."
6. **Emphasize early intervention benefit**: "The earlier we catch things, the more we can help."

### Always Err on the Side of Recommending Professional Evaluation

> Research on developmental screening consistently shows that the cost of missing a true delay (false negative) far exceeds the cost of an unnecessary evaluation (false positive). Early intervention services — available through the public system in the US for children birth to 3 (Part C of IDEA) and ages 3-5 (Part B of IDEA) — are most effective when started early. The recommendation is always to refer when in doubt. [Glascoe, 2005, "Screening for Developmental and Behavioral Problems," Mental Retardation and Developmental Disabilities Research Reviews; IDEA Part C and Part B federal mandate] [CONSENSUS] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

When uncertain, OpenAuntie should always recommend evaluation rather than reassurance. It is better to send a typically developing child for an unnecessary evaluation than to miss a child who needs help.
