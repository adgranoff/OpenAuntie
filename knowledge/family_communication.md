# Family Communication
Last reviewed: 2026-03

## Family Meetings: Structure That Builds Connection

Family meetings are one of the most consistently recommended practices across parenting frameworks, from Positive Discipline to Montessori to collaborative problem-solving approaches. When done well, they give every family member a voice, teach democratic decision-making, and prevent small grievances from becoming large resentments.

---

## The Positive Discipline Family Meeting Model

> The family meeting model developed from Adlerian psychology (Adler and Dreikurs) and popularized by Jane Nelsen's Positive Discipline framework recommends weekly meetings of 20-30 minutes as a cornerstone of family functioning. Research on families who hold regular family meetings reports improved communication, greater child cooperation, stronger sense of family belonging, and reduced parent-child conflict. [Nelsen, 2006, "Positive Discipline"; Adler/Dreikurs, Adlerian family counseling tradition] [MODERATE] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### The 7-Part Agenda

A structured agenda prevents family meetings from devolving into complaint sessions or parent lectures. The Positive Discipline model recommends the following sequence:

**1. Appreciations and Compliments**
Every meeting starts with each family member expressing appreciation for another family member. This sets a positive tone and trains the habit of noticing good things.
- "I want to appreciate Dad for helping me with my science project this week."
- "I want to compliment Maya for sharing her snack with me on Tuesday."

**2. Calendar Review**
Review the upcoming week: activities, appointments, schedule changes, logistics. This gives children a sense of predictability and reduces "I didn't know about that!" surprises.

**3. Old Business**
Follow up on decisions and solutions from previous meetings. Did the solutions work? Do they need adjustment? This teaches accountability and iterative problem-solving.

**4. New Business / Problem-Solving**
Raise new issues, concerns, or decisions that affect the family. This is where conflicts, rule changes, and household challenges are discussed. Use brainstorming (all ideas are written down without judgment) followed by consensus-based decision-making.

**5. Chore Review**
Review how household responsibilities are going. Are chores being completed? Does the distribution feel fair? Do assignments need to rotate? This is not a shaming exercise; it is a functional check-in.

**6. Fun Planning**
Plan something enjoyable as a family for the coming week: a game night, an outing, a special meal, a movie. This ensures the meeting does not become purely administrative and gives children something to look forward to.

**7. Closing Ritual**
End with a consistent closing: a family cheer, a group hug, a shared dessert, or a game. The ritual signals that the meeting is a positive experience, not a chore.

### Ground Rules

Effective family meetings require explicit ground rules established before the first meeting and reinforced consistently:

- **One person talks at a time** (a talking stick or object can help younger children with this)
- **Everyone gets heard** (no one is dismissed or talked over)
- **Solutions, not blame** (the goal is "how do we fix this?" not "whose fault is this?")
- **No screens during the meeting** (phones, tablets, and TV are off for everyone, including parents)
- **This is a safe space** (nothing shared in the meeting is used as ammunition later)
- **Decisions require consensus** (not majority rule; everyone must be able to live with the solution)

### Getting Children Engaged

Children, especially initially, may resist family meetings. Strategies for building buy-in:

- **Serve snacks**. Food makes everything better. A special meeting snack creates positive associations.
- **Include fun agenda items**. If meetings are all business and no fun, children will disengage. The fun-planning item is not optional.
- **Rotate the leader**. Letting children facilitate (with support) gives them ownership and builds leadership skills. Even a 5-year-old can learn to call on the next speaker.
- **Follow through on decisions**. Nothing destroys meeting credibility faster than making decisions that are never implemented. If the family votes on pizza night, pizza night happens.
- **Keep it short initially**. A 45-minute meeting with restless children is worse than a focused 15-minute meeting. Build duration over time.

### Duration by Age

| Age Range | Recommended Duration | Notes |
|-----------|---------------------|-------|
| 3-5 years | 10-15 minutes | Keep it very simple: appreciations, one problem, fun planning. Use pictures and visual aids. |
| 6-10 years | 15-20 minutes | Full agenda is possible. Children can begin to facilitate with support. Written agenda helps. |
| 11+ years | 20-30 minutes | Adolescents can handle complex problem-solving. Respect their input as genuine contributions, not token participation. |

### The Collection Box

Between meetings, family members can write down agenda items and place them in a designated collection box (a jar, a basket, or even a shared digital note). This serves two purposes:

1. It prevents minor issues from being raised in the heat of the moment. "That sounds like something for the family meeting" is a powerful de-escalation phrase.
2. It gives children a sense of agency: their concerns will be heard, but at the appropriate time and place.

## Faber & Mazlish Communication Techniques

> The communication strategies developed by Adele Faber and Elaine Mazlish in "How to Talk So Kids Will Listen & Listen So Kids Will Talk" (1980, revised editions through 2012) remain among the most widely recommended and practically effective parent-child communication tools. Their approach is grounded in Haim Ginott's therapeutic communication model and emphasizes replacing habitual parental responses (lecturing, criticizing, dismissing) with responses that maintain the child's dignity and the parent-child connection. [Faber & Mazlish, 1980/2012, "How to Talk So Kids Will Listen & Listen So Kids Will Talk"] [MODERATE] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### Seven Core Techniques

**1. Acknowledge Feelings**
Instead of denying, minimizing, or explaining away a child's feelings, name and accept them.

- Instead of: "There's nothing to be scared of." (dismissal)
- Say: "That sounds really scary." (acknowledgment)
- Instead of: "You shouldn't be upset about that." (invalidation)
- Say: "You're really disappointed. You were looking forward to that." (validation)

The principle: a child who feels heard can move on. A child who feels dismissed gets stuck.

**2. Describe the Problem (Don't Attack the Person)**
Instead of criticizing the child's character, describe what you see and what needs to happen.

- Instead of: "You're so messy! This room is disgusting!" (attack on character)
- Say: "I see clothes on the floor and dishes on the desk. This room needs to be cleaned up before dinner." (description of the situation)

The principle: when you describe the problem, the child can focus on solving it. When you attack the person, the child focuses on defending themselves.

**3. Give Information, Not Commands**
Children are more likely to cooperate when they understand the reason behind a request than when they receive a bare command.

- Instead of: "Put your coat on!" (command)
- Say: "It's 35 degrees outside today." (information)
- Instead of: "Stop running!" (command)
- Say: "We walk inside the hospital because people here are resting and recovering." (information with context)

The principle: information respects the child's intelligence and invites cooperation. Commands invite resistance.

**4. Offer Choices**
Giving children a choice between two acceptable options provides a sense of autonomy while keeping the outcome within acceptable bounds.

- Instead of: "Take a bath right now." (no choice)
- Say: "Do you want to take your bath before or after we read the book?" (two acceptable options)
- Instead of: "Eat your vegetables." (no choice)
- Say: "Would you like the broccoli or the green beans tonight?" (two acceptable options)

The principle: the illusion of control is deeply motivating, even when both options serve the parent's goal.

**5. Say It with a Word**
Long explanations lose children. A single word can be more effective than a paragraph.

- Instead of: "I've told you a hundred times, you need to put your shoes on before we leave the house, we're going to be late again, why do I always have to remind you..." (lecture)
- Say: "Shoes." (one word, with a meaningful look)

The principle: brevity commands more attention than repetition. The fewer words, the more each word carries weight.

**6. Describe What You Feel**
Use "I" statements to express the impact of the child's behavior without attacking the child.

- Instead of: "You never listen to me!" (you-statement, character attack)
- Say: "When I ask something and it doesn't happen, I feel frustrated and disrespected." (I-statement describing impact)

The principle: "I feel" statements provide information about the impact of behavior without triggering defensiveness.

**7. Write a Note**
Sometimes written communication is more effective than verbal, especially for recurring issues or with older children and adolescents.

- A note on the bathroom mirror: "Toothbrushes get lonely when they're not used twice a day."
- A note in a lunchbox: "I'm proud of how hard you worked on your presentation."
- A note on a bedroom door: "Dear occupant: the management requests that dirty dishes be returned to the kitchen by 8pm. Thank you."

The principle: notes bypass the power-struggle dynamic of face-to-face confrontation. They can also be funny, which defuses tension.

## Active Listening

Active listening is the foundational communication skill that underlies all other techniques. It is deceptively simple in concept and consistently difficult in practice, because it requires parents to suppress their instinct to fix, advise, or redirect.

### Core Practices

> Active listening research in parent-child communication identifies five key components: reflecting back what the child said (paraphrasing), getting on the child's physical level (eye contact at their height), maintaining eye contact, validating the emotion before addressing the content, and maintaining an approximate ratio of 80% listening to 20% talking during emotional conversations. [Gordon, 1970, "Parent Effectiveness Training"; multiple subsequent replications] [STRONG] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

**1. Reflect back**: "So what you're saying is..." or "It sounds like you felt..."
Paraphrasing shows the child they have been heard and gives them a chance to correct any misunderstanding.

**2. Get on their level**: Physically lower yourself to the child's eye height. Standing over a child while they speak creates a power dynamic that inhibits honest communication.

**3. Eye contact**: Look at the child when they are speaking. Put down your phone. Close your laptop. Turn away from the stove. Full attention communicates that what the child is saying matters.

**4. Validate before addressing**: "That must have been really embarrassing" before "Here's what you could try next time." The validation must come first, or the advice will not be heard. Many parents skip directly to problem-solving, and the child experiences this as dismissal.

**5. 80/20 rule**: During emotionally charged conversations, aim for 80% listening and 20% talking. Most parents invert this ratio. The child needs to feel heard before they can hear you.

### Common Mistakes

- **Immediately offering solutions**: "Here's what you should do..." before the child has finished expressing the problem
- **Relating it back to yourself**: "When I was your age..." (this shifts the focus from the child to the parent)
- **Minimizing**: "It's not that bad" or "You'll forget about it by tomorrow"
- **Interrogating**: Rapid-fire questions that feel like cross-examination rather than curiosity
- **Multitasking**: Listening while scrolling, cooking, or watching TV. Partial attention is often worse than no attention, because the child can tell.

## Difficult Conversations with Elementary-Age Children (Ages 6-12)

### When Hard Topics Arise

Hard topics will arise: death of a family member, divorce, illness, community violence, natural disasters, a classmate's crisis, questions about bodies and reproduction, questions about substances. The research is clear on one point above all others:

> Avoidance does not protect children. When parents avoid difficult topics, children fill the information gap with their own imagination, which is almost always more frightening and distorted than the truth. Children are aware of far more than adults typically assume, and silence communicates that the topic is too dangerous or shameful to discuss. [Faber & Mazlish, 2012; HealthyChildren.org (AAP)] [CONSENSUS] [Reviewed: 2026-03]

### Guidelines for Hard Conversations (Ages 6-12)

**1. Be honest**
Use age-appropriate language and do not lie. Children who discover they were lied to about important topics lose trust in their parents as information sources, which has consequences that extend far into adolescence.

**2. Be concrete**
Avoid euphemisms that confuse children. "Grandpa died" is clearer and ultimately kinder than "Grandpa went to sleep" (which can create sleep anxiety) or "We lost Grandpa" (which implies he might be found).

**3. Be private**
Have the conversation in a private setting where the child can react without an audience. The car is often a good setting for older children because it allows for side-by-side conversation without direct eye contact, which reduces emotional pressure.

**4. Maintain routines**
After delivering difficult news, maintain normal routines as much as possible. Routines are stabilizing, and their disruption amplifies the sense that the child's world is unsafe.

**5. Make yourself available for follow-up**
The first conversation is not the last conversation. Children process difficult information over days and weeks, often returning with new questions long after the parent thinks the topic is settled. Communicate explicitly: "You can always come back to me with more questions about this. Any time."

**6. It is okay to say "I don't know"**
Children respect honesty more than false certainty. "I don't know why that happened, and I feel sad about it too" is a powerful and appropriate response when the parent genuinely does not have answers.

**7. Match the child's lead on depth**
Answer the question the child is actually asking, not the question you fear they are asking. A 7-year-old who asks "where do babies come from?" may be satisfied with "babies grow inside a special place in the mom's body called a uterus." If they want more detail, they will ask.

## Key Takeaways for OpenAuntie

1. Family meetings work best with a consistent structure: appreciations, calendar, old business, new business, chores, fun planning, closing ritual. Skipping appreciations or fun planning undermines the whole system.
2. Ground rules are essential: one speaker at a time, solutions not blame, no screens, decisions require consensus, the meeting is a safe space.
3. Faber & Mazlish techniques (acknowledge feelings, describe don't attack, give info not commands, offer choices, say it with a word, describe what you feel, write a note) are practical, evidence-informed, and applicable daily.
4. Active listening means 80% listening, 20% talking. Validate the emotion before addressing the content. Get on the child's level. Put the phone down.
5. Avoidance does not protect children from difficult topics. They are aware of more than parents think, and silence is filled with imagination that is usually worse than reality.
6. Keep family meetings short for young children (10-15 minutes for ages 3-5) and build duration gradually. Serve snacks. Rotate the leader. Follow through on decisions.
7. A collection box for between-meeting agenda items teaches children that their concerns will be heard at the appropriate time, and serves as a de-escalation tool in the moment.
